Chapter I. - The Ruins of Turayne
Turayne had once been a city that thrived thanks to the Grassway — an ancient trade route lazily winding between mountains and forests. For centuries, it had been protected by a lineage of powerful mages who devoted their lives to its safety. But on the day of the funeral of the last High Mage, everything changed.
That was the day the Shadow Mages of the Sylvean Order attacked. War erupted — cruel and brief — in which the city’s remaining mages fought in vain against the invaders. Turayne fell, and nothing remained of the city but ruins.
The stone arches of the old Temple of Tevent jutted toward the sky like petrified fingers of gods who had once ruled this land. The entrance hall, once bright and full of prayers, was now hollow and swallowed by shadow. Time did not flow here — it gnawed at everything like mold. Dust covered every surface so thickly that each step stirred swirling clouds that seemed to whisper the remnants of ancient prayers.
The pillars were cracked, some collapsed, others overgrown with black lichens that pulsed with their own dark energy. The air carried the stench of rotting flesh and something indescribable that settled on the palate like ash.
From every alcove stared a statue — but the eyes had been gouged out, the faces torn apart. Some were broken beyond recognition, making it impossible to tell whom or what they had once depicted.
Among the rubble lay bodies — decayed, half-consumed by the stone floor. Some did not move. Others… moved far too slowly.
The undead.
Silent, twisted, as if they had long forgotten what their limbs were meant for. They reached out blindly, without eyes, yet they still sensed life. The temple had not spat them out — it held them. They all remained here. Priests, pilgrims, healers… their souls trapped in the walls, in the floor, in the shadows. Waiting.
Wind slipped through the shattered windows, but it carried no fresh air — only cold and the whisper of long-buried things that were never meant to awaken again. In some parts of the temple, a strange echo lingered, belonging to no step and no voice. As if something were listening. Something that had not fully fallen asleep.
From the distance came the crunch of debris — quiet, yet foreign. A step. Another step. Then another.
In the dark passage between two collapsed pillars, a silhouette flickered for a moment. A sliver of light from a broken window fell upon a figure moving slowly and quietly, as if examining every shadow and every crack around her. She looked as though she were searching for something — or making sure she was alone.
Her long, straight black hair swayed softly with each step, framing her pale skin like a dark waterfall. Her black eyes, deep and calm as the night sky, held both determination and quiet sorrow. The blue dress she wore was marked by travel and dust, yet still carried traces of elegance.
Rowena came from Kastleton, a bustling port city that lived to the pulsing rhythm of ships, markets, and trade. The scent of sea salt mingled with the aroma of spices, exotic herbs, and fresh fruits brought from distant lands. The streets were filled with the cries of merchants offering fabrics, precious jewelry, and mysterious artifacts. Kastleton was a place where cultures, languages, and destinies from across the world met.
Rowena loved the city with all her heart — it was not just a place of business opportunities, but a home that gave her a sense of safety and freedom. Her family had always been her support; her parents, experienced merchants, managed a renowned family business dealing in medicinal herbs, many of which came from the surrounding forests. Kastleton meant not only the place of her childhood, but roots that kept her grounded even when her path led far away.
When she was eight years old, an old healer visited the city and sensed something extraordinary within her. He realized that Rowena possessed a rare gift for healing magic. After a long conversation, her parents agreed that the girl should follow her destiny, and the healer took her to the Tower of Pure Light — a prestigious academy where magic was taught in its purest and most powerful forms.
In the Tower, she studied healing rituals, ancient scrolls, and the secrets of the Garden of Peace, which would one day make her capable of helping the suffering. Every day she trained diligently, improving her abilities while carrying within her both the weight of responsibility and hope.
She gripped a wand in her hand, adorned with delicate runes, and at her belt hung a magical book — an old volume filled with rituals that could change the fate of many. Each step she took in the Temple of Tevent felt like sinking into a swamp of memories — a place where past and present merged into a single dark current. Every breath she drew was like the cry of those who had died here long ago and yet still called for help.
Still, she moved forward — for the scrolls, for hope. Because even where everything has long since died, the key to saving the living may still lie hidden. Her mind was sharp, her heart full of faith that magic was not only a weapon, but a light capable of illuminating even the darkest corners of the world.
Then she felt it.
Not the chill of the wind, but another kind — the kind that runs down your spine when something without eyes is watching you.
Specters.
Temple priests — shapeless shadows of the past, without faces or words. But with an insatiable hunger to drain the last spark of light from every living being.
Ten. Too close. Too fast.
Rowena stepped back, one step at a time, as ten specters slowly surrounded her. Their faceless heads tilted, as if studying her every movement, every trace of her magic. The hands of the undead stretched toward her — fingers long and thin as needles, reaching to seize and drain her life force.
She clutched an amulet with a white crystal, cold as ice, meant to give her strength. But her breath caught, her heart pounded wildly in her chest, and a desperate thought flashed through her mind:
I can’t do this…
She knew their number was too great, their speed deadly.
Suddenly, one of the specters lunged forward. Its decaying, translucent body shot through the air with terrifying speed, its claws rushing toward her throat.
In that instant, something unexpected happened.
The world exploded in blinding light.
A massive pillar of fire tore through the temple roof — a beam of sunlight transformed into a raging flame that shattered the stone vault like the furious wrath of a deity. The fire instantly engulfed the specter; its translucent body began to dissolve in the radiance, as if melting in the heat of pure energy. From its mouth came a scream that vanished into the thunder of flames.
The remaining nine specters attacked at once, but the fire around Rowena formed an impenetrable circle. Sparks fell onto the stone floor, illuminating the dark corners, while the specters hissed loudly and fled from the force that destroyed their undead forms.
The air filled with the smell of burning. Heat flooded the temple, which suddenly seemed alive in the glow of flames. Sparks danced around her, lighting her tense face.
When the smoke slowly began to clear, another figure appeared in the glow of fire, standing upon a stone pedestal.
A woman with red, wavy hair falling to her shoulder blades.
Her eyes were just as red — piercing and unyielding, as if they carried flames of unquenchable passion and unwavering courage. Her lightly tanned skin, kissed by the sun, only emphasized an expression that was both sensual and dangerous — beauty that was not merely decoration, but a weapon.
She wore red, oriental-style robes — light and flowing, made of fine fabric that moved like hot wind over desert dunes. They were loose where they needed to cool the body and fitted where beauty was meant to stand out — with elegant folds and slits allowing effortless movement while still appearing sensual. The fabric carried a delicate, almost invisible pattern of waves and wind.
At first glance, it was clear that such garments did not come from cold lands — but from Vienta, a port city on the edge of the desert known as the Monolithic Wasteland. A city where the sun never fully sets and hot wind dances through sandstone streets.
Vienta is a city of heat — not only because of its location, but because of what flows within its people: fire magic.
In Vienta, flame is not considered dangerous — but a blessing. Fire is a symbol of life, motion, and transformation. It is mastered from childhood, not as a weapon, but as a living force that can protect, empower, and create. Their magic is as beautiful as it is destructive, as precise as it is passionate.
The city’s greatest temple — the Sanctuary of the Crimson Breath — is devoted to fire as a purifying force. There, students learn not only spells, but discipline and philosophy: that fire, like emotion, must be guided by a steady hand — otherwise it will consume everything. True power lies not in destruction, but in control, passion, and determination.
The garments worn by its mages are not merely clothing — they represent balance between strength and beauty, between chaos and calm, between destructive force and the will to master it.
And she was exactly that.
Her stance was firm and confident, her gaze sharp and unyielding. Every movement radiated strength and charisma that was impossible to ignore — she was a woman who not only loved trouble, but who was never afraid to face it with fire in her heart and passion in her soul.
“That wasn’t very elegant, was it?” came a lightly teasing voice.
