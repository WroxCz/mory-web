Chapter VI. - Cracks in the armor
The desert was silent again.
But the silence was not peaceful — it was hollow, heavy, stretched tight like a string before it snaps. The creature that had risen from the depths now lay lifeless in the dust. Where bones had once been, only a glimmering mosaic remained, shattered beneath the night sky like a broken dream.
Aaliyah stood there, breathless. Her shoulders trembled with every inhale. The air around her still carried the fading remnants of magic — fiery, hissing, corrosive. Her final spell had been lethal. It had left behind only silence and scorched earth.
James stood a short distance away, silent, his spear still in hand. The blade was stained with silvery ash, yet he did not move. As if he expected the creature to rise again. As if this victory were only an illusion. His body was covered in dust and sweat, but the expression on his face was empty.
Rowena stood beside Aaliyah, her palms pressed against her wounds. Her spells were like soft threads of light, slipping into bleeding cuts and sealing them before they could leave scars. Healing magic pulsed between them, steady and calm — like a mother’s touch. Her hands trembled, not from exhaustion, but from what she had seen in the creature’s eyes just before it fell apart.
There had not been emptiness there.
There had been pain.
Rowena closed her eyes. “She… was suffering,” she whispered, barely audible.
Aaliyah inhaled slowly. The fire around her quieted, drawing closer to her body, as if listening.
“That doesn’t change anything,” she replied after a moment, her voice hard.
“It would have killed us.”
“I know,” Rowena nodded. The light between her palms brightened, then slowly calmed.
Aaliyah turned to James.
“That thing you were tracking,” she said, her voice sharpening, “before you reached us… was it this?”
“Yes. It attacked a caravan not far from here,” James answered calmly.
He crouched closer, one hand resting on his knee, his eyes steady but focused.
“They hired me,” he began quietly, “when the caravan was delayed for several days and no message came. They feared something had happened, so I went to meet them.”
For a moment, he lowered his gaze, as if piecing the memory together again.
“First I found the wagon tracks. Then scattered goods. The canvas was torn, the wood splintered… and there was blood everywhere.”
He paused.
“I thought it was one of the desert beasts.” A short breath. “Then I found the bodies. The bite marks didn’t match any creature that lives in this desert.”
He looked up.
“In that mouth… there were too many teeth,” he said slowly. “They were packed together, crooked, uneven. As if they didn’t belong there.”
He reached into his pocket. For a moment, he hesitated — then pulled out a tooth.
It was long and curved, at first glance belonging to a sand lizard. But not quite. The surface was twisted, deformed, veined with fine black cracks.
James straightened and stood. His movement was calm but decisive. Without a word, he handed the tooth to Aaliyah.
She took it and examined it briefly.
“You’re right. Sand lizards don’t have teeth like this,” she said calmly, with certainty.
“Their jaws are simple. This is… wrong.”
Without hesitation, she passed the tooth to Rowena.
The moment it touched her palm, she flinched slightly. The light surrounding her shuddered and thickened, as if colliding with something unwilling to yield. She closed her eyes and focused only on the sensation.
“I can feel dark magic in it,” she said softly. “It’s old. Very old.”
She opened her eyes, unease in her gaze.
“I’ve never felt anything like this.”
James nodded. “I felt the same in the caravan,” he replied quietly.
Aaliyah crossed her arms and stared into the darkness of the desert for a moment.
“It’s not wise to travel in the dark. We’ll wait until morning,” she said firmly. Then her expression hardened.
“But let’s be clear about one thing.”
The flames crackled softly, as if reacting to the change in her tone.
“This isn’t just a stray beast. If old dark magic is involved — if someone awakened or created something like this…”She paused briefly. “We should report it.”
“In Vienta?” Rowena asked.
“Yes. Or at the nearest oasis,” Aaliyah nodded. “Caravans pass through here all the time. And this thing doesn’t kill out of hunger.”
James remained silent for a moment, then slowly nodded.
“I agree. First we find where the tracks lead. And if there’s more than one creature…”
“…then it should be reported at Stonegard Castle,” Rowena finished quietly.
The silence that followed was no longer filled only with fear.
It was filled with decision.
Morning would not bring only light — it would bring direction.
James straightened and looked calmly at both women.
“Go to sleep,” he said quietly. “I’ll take the first watch. If anything moves, I’ll wake you.”
Aaliyah shot him a brief look, as if considering an argument, then snorted softly in annoyance.
“Fine. But if you let some ancient horror eat you, I’ll blame you for it even after you’re dead.”
Rowena nodded, still slightly lost in thought.
“Wake us at the first sign of danger,” she said quietly. “Whether it’s a sound… or a feeling.”
James simply nodded.
“A shadow in the sand will be enough.”
The women went to rest while he remained sitting by the fire. The night was cold, and the desert unusually quiet. James placed his spear beside him on the ground, his eyes fixed on the darkness — alert, focused, ready should anything move around them.
Morning was approaching, but the sun had not yet risen, and the desert remained wrapped in darkness.
James sat by the fire, sharpening his dagger. His movements were precise, rhythmic — almost too calm. The fingers on his left hand were bandaged, the cloth stained in places with dried blood. His breathing was shallow, controlled.
Rowena was the first to wake.
Quietly, almost imperceptibly.
She sat up, glanced around briefly, and her gaze immediately settled on James’s bandaged fingers. She said nothing. She simply reached for her satchel and stood.
“I’ll try to find some herbs,” she whispered.
Then she disappeared into the dim light.
A short while later, Aaliyah awoke. She inhaled the cold morning air deeply, her eyes sharpening instantly — no drowsiness, only immediate alertness. She noticed Rowena’s absence, then the bandages on James’s hand.
Without a word, she moved closer to the flames and sat opposite him. She watched him silently, her gaze sharp and attentive.
“You’re playing invincible again,” she said quietly.
“I don’t want anyone to pity me,” he replied without looking up.
“No one pities you,” she answered. “But maybe… someone is simply afraid that it no longer matters to you whether you live or die.”
He looked at her.
And this time, the look lingered.
“And you, Aaliyah?” he asked. “Does anything still matter to you… at all?”
She wanted to answer. Fiercely. Quickly. With a joke.
But instead, she lowered her eyes.
“I don’t know,” she admitted.
James stood, walked slowly around the fire, and sat beside her. Then he placed his hand on her shoulder.
Not firmly. Not with certainty.
More like… he was doing it for the first time.
Rowena stood at a distance.
The wind tangled her long black hair, and the skirt of her dark blue dress rustled around her ankles, but she did not move. In her hands she held healing herbs, yet she had forgotten why she had gone to fetch them.
Before her, by the fire, sat Aaliyah — and beside her, James.
She watched their eyes meet. She saw Aaliyah smile in that slightly uncertain way of hers, as if trying to ease the silence around them. And she saw that James, a man who often seemed to speak only with his eyes, heard that smile.
Rowena sighed.
There was no jealousy in it.
Only concern.
Aaliyah was like fire — bright, warm, untamed. But fire is vulnerable. It needs air, and if it receives too little, it dies.
And James?
Rowena studied him.
He was not the kind of man who opened himself easily. His calm was deceptive — the silence of a man who carried his pain in his bones, who was used to being alone so he would not lose anyone.
What if he doesn’t hurt her on purpose? she thought.
What if he won’t even realize he’s hurting her?
Aaliyah had a tendency to give all of herself. And Rowena knew she did not always keep anything back.
She feared not the man sitting beside her friend — but what Aaliyah might be willing to sacrifice just to keep him from leaving.
Rowena lowered her gaze to the plants in her hands.
She understood wounds that bled.
But the ones in the heart?
Those healed more slowly.
And if that pain came, it would not be James who gathered the shattered pieces.
It would be her.
That was all right.
She would do it again and again, whenever necessary.
Because Aaliyah was her friend.
Not an ideal. Not a dream.
But a living person who had helped others so many times that she deserved someone who would finally watch over her.
Rowena placed the herbs back into her satchel and walked toward the fire. The two of them lifted their heads.
“You should eat something,” she said calmly. “It’s only going to get worse.”
Aaliyah smiled at her.
And Rowena returned the smile.
