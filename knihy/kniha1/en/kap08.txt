Chapter VIII. - The key between times
The temple was changing in silence.
The walls, once bare and crumbling, now carried imprints. Not of images — but of memories. They could be read only by those who were not afraid to look back. Hands reaching into emptiness. Faces frozen in screams. Palms clutching what had been lost.
And among it all — mist.
Not merely dampness, but a fog of thoughts. It was heavy, cloying, familiar and yet foreign. It drifted downward from cracks in the ceiling, as if seeping from another layer of existence. It carried the taste of the past and the scent of guilt.
Every breath burned like a confession.
Rowena stood in silence.
Her eyes were fixed on one of the walls, where a scene slowly formed: a girl running through a dense forest, breath ragged, heart pounding in her throat.
She recognized the sight.
She knew it was her.
And she knew what would come next.
The image was not just an image — it was a return.
Painful. Inevitable.
When Aaliyah appeared beside her, no footsteps were heard. Only presence. As if she, too, were nothing more than a shadow.
“You know what to do, don’t you?” Aaliyah finally broke the silence. “I can see it in your eyes.”
Rowena nodded.
“Yes. We have to go through.”
“Through the image? Those shadows on the walls?” Aaliyah asked, her gaze darting across the flickering silhouettes.
“They’re not just shadows,” Rowena whispered, reaching out toward one of them. Her fingers brushed the wall, and the shadow trembled.
“They are memories. Fragments that remained… or were drawn out of us. Each piece is a key.”
Aaliyah shuddered, her eyes scanning the other scenes. Her face paled when she glimpsed her own past.
“This isn’t remembering,” she whispered. “This is… real.”
“Memories here are not dead,” Rowena replied. “They are alive. And they are holding us here.”
“How… do you know what this is?” Aaliyah asked. Her voice cracked.
Rowena tore her gaze away from the scene and looked at her.
“I found a record. Not in a book, not on a scroll. In a sealed chest in the Archive, deep beneath the main hall. It was a thought placed inside a magical orb. A trace left by someone who studied Fragments of Time for years — and never returned from one.”
She lowered her voice.
“He told me that memories are not the past. They are keys. And that if we close our eyes to them, the temple will never let us go.”
Aaliyah stepped closer to the wall.
She touched it.
A scene formed upon the stone — like something from a distant dream, or a nightmare no one wished to witness.
In a graveyard stood many figures, blurred like shadows buried in the fog of time. Some were barely visible, others sharp and clear.
At the center stood a man in a dark cloak, tall and upright — Lord Hobbs Kastler. His face was stern, yet filled with quiet sorrow. In his hands he held an urn.
It was simple.
But it carried the weight of the world’s grief.
The urn belonged to his son, Henry — the boy who had sacrificed himself.
Henry, who on the day of the dark wolves’ attack did not hesitate. He faced the danger to save the other children. His courage cost him his life — but it also left behind hope that survived.
Near Lord Hobbs Kastler, barely visible among the shadows, stood a small girl — Rowena, still a child.
Her face was pale, almost translucent. Her eyes empty, filled with pain. She stood apart, as if wanting to exist and disappear at the same time.
She was one of those Henry saved.
One of those who survived — but with a soul forever marked by loss.
Her gaze was distant, as though she were watching a stranger’s story.
Yet it was the truth she could not escape.
The entire image pulsed with sorrow. It screamed silently, demanding acceptance.
This was not remembering.
It was a vow to truth — one many refused to face.
“This… is you?”
“Yes,” Rowena nodded.
“If you refuse to face your memories and keep denying them, you will remain in this temple forever. You will not move forward. Because this place feeds on our denial.”
At that moment, the room fell silent — and the silence turned heavy, suffocating.
It was not merely a drop in temperature. It was a cold that seeped beneath the skin. A pressure that tightened the chest and slowed the breath.
As if the walls themselves were holding back a scream.
The shadows began to tremble.
They shifted, thickened, gained weight.
From vague outlines, reality was born.
They were no longer mere silhouettes — they were traces of actions, records of faces, echoes of words once spoken, still carrying the burden of decisions.
Every movement, every ripple of darkness, reminded them of the price of the past.
The air in the temple grew dense.
And with it came the realization that time here did not move forward — but in circles.
That every unspoken moment returned, sharp as a blade.
“We have to go now,” Rowena said. “Before the keys fall apart.”
Aaliyah swallowed.
“And what if… we get lost?”
“Then we will find each other,” Rowena answered. “But first, we must go alone.”
Silence.
Then a step.
One.
Another.
Heavy. Determined.
A figure emerged from the shadows.
James stood apart, hidden in darkness, a silent observer of everything unfolding around him. His eyes followed the movement of memories — the faces and stories on the walls — as if trying to understand every detail, every pain, every scream.
Then he stepped forward slowly.
He entered one of the flickering shadows.
His figure began to dissolve, as though he had become part of the memory — the pain, the past itself.
Just before he vanished, he turned slightly and whispered:
“We’ll meet on the other side.”
The temple trembled.
The mist rose.
Rowena drew a breath.
And stepped forward.
The wall swallowed her.
Without a sound. Without a trace.
Only Aaliyah remained.
And her own past.
She took a step.
And the temple burst into light.

Searching for the Key — Rowena’s Perspective
Leaves. Air. Fear.
Rowena ran.
Her feet sank into the soil, branches scratching her arms. Behind her ran the other children — four girls and three boys.
All the same age.
All equally lost.
And behind them — a sound.
Not barking.
Something worse.
The howl of wolves.
Soon they saw them.
They were darker than shadows, with glowing red eyes.
“Keep moving!” shouted the boy in front.
Henry.
The son of Lord Hobbs Kastler.
Fear filled his eyes — but so did determination. His steps were steady. His short brown hair was tousled by the wind, his fine clothes already torn and muddy as he ran beside the others.
At his belt hung a short sword.
A gift for his twelfth birthday, the family crest engraved into its hilt.
“Run!” Henry shouted.
Suddenly he stopped and, in one smooth motion, drew his sword.
Not like a child.
Like a warrior who had already accepted his role.
There was no fear in his eyes.
“No!” Rowena screamed, but she knew she could no longer stop him.
Henry looked back over his shoulder.
A smile.
Faint — but calm.
His eyes spoke the words he did not say:
You must live.
The darkness parted.
More wolves emerged from the undergrowth — six, maybe eight. Larger than dogs. Heavier. Each step left behind a shadow.
Their teeth gleamed in the blackness of their jaws.
Henry stepped forward.
Left foot slightly ahead of the right — as if he were dancing.
He raised his sword.
A growl.
Deep.
Heavy.
Like the echo of death.
The first wolf leapt.
Henry struck — elegant, precise. His blade hit its mark. The wolf howled and disappeared into the grass.
But more followed.
Too many.
Rowena kept running.
Tears streamed down her face, but she did not allow herself to slow.
She knew that if she turned around, something inside her would break forever.

The scene changed.
Rowena found herself standing in a graveyard, still in her child’s form.
Before her stood Henry.
The boy who had saved her.
Henry spoke in a quiet but steady voice:
“You didn’t leave me there. Stop blaming yourself.”
Rowena burst into tears.
“I should have stayed with you.”
“No, you shouldn’t have. I wanted you to live — and I still do. I’m glad we could meet again. But it’s time for you to move on.”
Henry stepped closer and embraced her.
“This isn’t goodbye. I will always watch over you.”
Her body trembled.
She did not want to leave him.
Not again.
But at last, she stepped toward the doors of the Kastler family crypt.
Rowena appeared once more inside the temple.
But this time, it looked slightly different.
As if with every accepted shadow, it was losing its power.
Yet it was far from finished.
