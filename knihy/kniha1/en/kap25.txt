Chapter XXV. - In the silence of the heart
Thomas had been on edge all day.
After the trio left with DaVinci to meet Commander Hander and High Mage Parsons, he had been forced to return to his post. He stood on the battlements, watching the courtyard, the gates, and the desert dunes, yet his thoughts kept drifting elsewhere.
To Rowena.
He had seen her only for a moment. Exhausted, shaken… and yet there was also that image that burrowed under his skin more deeply than he wanted to admit.
That man.
The way he held her. The way he leaned too close to her.
Thomas told himself it had been necessary, that it meant nothing — but reason carried less weight than the feeling in his chest.
His watch ended only with the coming of evening. Once the castle sank into dim twilight and torches took command of the light, Thomas returned to his chamber. He removed his armor, bathed, and dressed in clean clothes.
Still, peace did not come.
And so, when night fell, Thomas quietly left his chamber and made his way down the stone-lined corridor that led to Rowena’s room. Each of his steps was firm, yet his heart beat faster than he expected.
He felt this was not an ordinary visit — it was a moment he had been waiting for, for months.
He stopped before her door.
For a moment he hesitated and glanced around to make sure the corridor was empty. Then he knocked softly, carefully.
After a moment, the door opened.
Rowena stood there, dressed in a loose nightgown made of soft fabric.
Thomas froze.
His gaze instinctively slipped downward — to the loose nightgown — and then quickly returned to her face.
“I’m sorry,” he breathed quietly, startled by his own reaction.
Rowena was just as surprised by his arrival. Something flickered in her eyes — somewhere between joy and embarrassment. The realization that she stood in the doorway wearing only a nightgown made her stiffen slightly.
“I… I’ll just get my robe,” she said quickly, stepping back into the room.
Without waiting, she turned and almost hurried inside.
Thomas remained standing in the doorway for only a fraction of a second, then followed her. As soon as he stepped in, he closed the door behind him — quietly, but firmly.
He did not want anyone to see them together in the corridor. Not today. Not like this.
Rowena slipped a robe over her shoulders and tied it more tightly around herself.
When she turned back to him, it was clear she felt a little embarrassed… yet also glad he was there.
A brief silence settled between them.
Close. Unusual. And yet familiar.
“I…” Thomas began, then stopped. In the end, he only gave a faint smile. “I suppose I came late.”
Rowena shook her head.
“No,” she answered softly. “Just… unexpectedly.”
Thomas studied her silently for a moment. Not as if examining her — more with a quiet worry he could not hide.
Even in the soft lamplight, it was clear she was still pale, still tired, her shoulders slightly slumped, as though the weight of the past days had not yet lifted from her.
“You still look… very worn out,” he said quietly. “Did you go to the infirmary?”
Rowena nodded.
“Yes. The healers said it’s mostly exhaustion. Nothing serious. Healers don’t have much endurance.”
She tried to smile weakly, but it did not quite succeed.
Thomas clenched his jaw.
“I worried about you all day,” he admitted bluntly. “I had to come.”
That honesty lingered in the air between them.
Rowena lowered her gaze, but this time not out of embarrassment.
“I’m glad you’re here,” she said softly.
They stood in silence for a moment. Then Thomas drew a quiet breath, as if trying to change the direction of the conversation — or simply gather his thoughts.
“That man,” he said carefully at last. “The one who was holding you…”
Rowena lifted her head. “James.”
Thomas did not like the name — not even the calm way it was spoken.
“Who is he?” he asked. Not sharply. More restrained. “And where did you meet him?”
Rowena sat down at the table and wrapped her hands around a cup of water left there from dinner.
“He’s a mercenary,” she answered. “He traveled alone for a long time. We met on the road through the desert… if he hadn’t been there, I might not be standing here now.”
She looked directly at Thomas.
“He helped us. More than once.”
Thomas nodded slowly.
He accepted the information, even if it did not sit well with him.
“So he belongs to them,” he said more to himself. “To them?” Rowena repeated, confused. “What do you mean?”
Thomas hesitated for a moment, then answered calmly, without sharpness:
“To hired mercenaries. To people who fight for coin, not for oath.”
Rowena frowned.
She did not like those words — and it showed.
“That’s not fair,” she said quietly but firmly. “Maybe he fights for coin, but that doesn’t mean he has no honor.”
She looked straight into Thomas’s eyes.
“James could have left. More than once,” she continued. “No one would have blamed him. And yet he stayed. He risked his life. For us.”
Her fingers tightened slightly around the cup.
“Those are not the decisions of a man who only cares about payment,” she added calmly.
Thomas stiffened for a moment, and something harder appeared in his posture — the cold arrogance of a knight raised his whole life within a different order of the world.
“You think better of them than they deserve,” he said calmly, but the earlier softness had left his voice. “I know mercenaries. I’ve seen what they leave behind when the coin runs out.”
He fell silent, his gaze lingering on her a fraction longer than necessary.
“The way you speak about him…” he added quietly. “As if you care about him.”
The words slipped out before he could stop them.
“I care that he protected us,” she answered. “And that he never lied to us. That’s all.”
Thomas lowered his head slightly, but irritation flashed in his eyes.
“That’s more than I would expect from someone who fights for money,” he said sharply.
Those words were spoken from pride. From the conviction that had been instilled in him from the moment he first put on armor.
Rowena drew a slow breath, as if trying to remain calm. Her fingers tightened around the cup again — this time not from uncertainty, but from quiet defiance.
“Maybe,” she said softly but firmly, “you just see the world too much in black and white.”
She lifted her gaze to him.
There was no anger in it. Only fatigue and disappointment.
“You see oath and armor,” she continued. “I saw a man who always stepped forward to protect us with his own life.”
She fell silent for a moment, as if carefully weighing her next words.
“It doesn’t matter to me who he is according to the orders,” she added calmly. “What matters to me is who he was when lives were at stake.”
Thomas straightened. In his posture, pride mixed with something he did not want to admit. “And what if you’re wrong?” he asked. “What if one day he accepts an offer that’s more profitable?”
“Then that will be his choice,” she replied without hesitation. “Just as it is your choice to believe that honor belongs only to those who wear the right colors.”
Those words struck him.
For a moment, he said nothing. In the room, the only sound was the faint crack of wood in the fireplace, still holding the last traces of warmth.
“I’m trying to protect you,” he said at last, more quietly. “I don’t want you to let someone close who could betray you.”
Rowena’s gaze softened.
“I know,” she answered. “And that’s exactly why I’m telling you this honestly.”
She took a step closer and carefully touched his hand. Not firmly — just with the tips of her fingers, as if testing whether the same warmth still flowed between them.
Thomas stiffened — only for a moment. Then he took her hand into his palms, gently. Slowly, he lifted it to his lips and kissed the back of her hand.
Softly. Respectfully. The way he was used to showing feelings he took seriously.
He held her hand for another moment, then slowly released it.
“I trust your judgment,” he said calmly. And the words sounded exactly as they should — reasonable, balanced, mature. “If you say that man is honorable… then I believe you.”
Rowena looked at him, slightly surprised.
“Thank you,” she said quietly.
Thomas nodded.
“We won’t talk about him anymore,” he added.
Outwardly, it sounded like the matter was settled.
But inside… inside, it was different.
Beneath the calm mask, his blood was boiling. The image of James kept returning to him again and again — his closeness, the way he stood beside Rowena, the way he held her.
He could not believe in her judgment for even a moment.
Not because he didn’t trust her.
But because he knew the world of men without oaths too well.
Rowena noticed that the tension in his shoulders had not fully eased.
“Are you all right?” she asked carefully.
“Yes,” he answered immediately. And this time, he lied only halfway.
He reached out and gently brushed a strand of hair from her forehead — a brief, quiet gesture of closeness.
“I’m just glad you’re safe.”
Rowena did not stop his hand. On the contrary — for a moment, she leaned lightly into the touch, as if wanting to rest against it.
“I am,” she said softly. “Now I am.”
Thomas leaned closer and gently pressed his forehead against hers. He closed his eyes.
For a moment, everything else disappeared — the castle, duties, the thoughts that had weighed on him all day. Only that quiet, fragile moment remained between them. Her breath. Her warmth.
Rowena did not move. She simply exhaled softly, and her hand carefully touched his arm.
“I’ll stay a little longer,” he said quietly. “If you don’t mind.”
Rowena shook her head.
“I don’t mind.”
They remained standing like that for several seconds. Maybe longer. Time dissolved in the silence.
Then Thomas slowly opened his eyes, but did not pull his forehead away from hers.
“I missed you,” he said softly.
Rowena drew a gentle breath.
“I missed you too,” she answered just as quietly.
Thomas let her words linger between them for another moment. Then he slowly pulled back and took a deep breath.
“I should return to my chamber,” he said quietly. “I have watch duty in the morning.”
Rowena nodded. She knew him too well. She knew he would not stay — he took his role as a knight very seriously. Duty, watch, order, and responsibility were rooted deep inside him. They were the axis around which his entire world turned.
It saddened her.
But she had accepted it.
Rowena looked at him once more.
“Go,” she said softly. “So you can be rested in the morning.”
Thomas nodded, grateful for how easy things always were with her.
“Good night.”
“Good night.”

When the door closed quietly behind him, Rowena remained standing in the middle of the room. Alone.
Slowly, she pulled her robe tighter around her body and took a deep breath. She walked slowly to the window and looked out into the night courtyard.
Torches burned steadily. Guards changed shifts at precise intervals. The world continued according to the order Thomas knew so well.
She rested her forehead against the cold glass and closed her eyes for a moment.
She did not cry.
She simply allowed herself to feel the emptiness that came every time she chose to wait again.
“One day,” she whispered, barely audible. “One day, perhaps we will finally be together.”
Then she stepped away from the window, extinguished the lamp, and let the room sink into shadow.

Aaliyah could not sleep.
Her mind was far too restless.
The chamber was larger than she had expected. Too large. And too quiet.
The fire in the fireplace flickered, and the silk curtains softened the night chill, yet Aaliyah felt… out of place. Alone. Not peacefully alone, but uncomfortably — almost oppressively so.
It was not the first time she had a bed to herself — but for many months she had grown unaccustomed to such solitude.
She and Rowena had traveled across the world together, sharing tents, rooms, and silence by the fire. She was used to feeling her presence, hearing her breathing or whispered words just before falling asleep.
But now Rowena was somewhere else.
And Aaliyah was left alone with her thoughts.
She tossed and turned, sat for a while, paced the room. At last, she threw a cloak over her shoulders, slipped into soft shoes, and quietly opened the door.
The stone corridors of Stonegard felt different at night. As if the castle breathed in another rhythm — slower, yet uneasy. Her footsteps sounded louder, the shadows were longer, and the silence heavier.
She had no destination.
She simply needed movement. Space. Air.
And there, in one of the dim corridors, just around a corner touched only by pale moonlight, she met him.
William.
