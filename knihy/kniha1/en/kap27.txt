Chapter XXVII. – The hunt
When Aaliyah closed the door to her room, she leaned against it wearily and closed her eyes for a moment. Inside her, there was silence — not emptiness, but calm after a storm. The words James had spoken echoed in her mind.
And yet… she knew the peace in Stonegard was only temporary.
While Aaliyah rested quietly, James returned to the courtyard. His gaze was fixed on the darkness above, where the stars shone with faint, dim light. In his heart, he felt conflicting emotions. Happiness, because Aaliyah had returned his feelings — that quiet but steady bond between them gave him strength and hope after everything they had endured together.
At the same time, anger and hatred toward William grew inside him — the man who had complicated their lives so many times and left deep scars. In his thoughts, James could not help a bitter wish:
If only he had never been born.
With clenched fists, he silently vowed to do everything in his power to protect Aaliyah and everyone he loved — no matter the danger that still lay ahead.

Zarveth walked through the desert.
His body was weakened, but his spirit remained unbroken. The darkness that kept him alive vibrated with a single desire: to fulfill its purpose — to free his master.
But he could not do it without allies.
He needed another Shadow.
And another body to serve as a vessel.
Then he heard voices.
Muted. Close.
He stopped on the crest of a dune and watched the camp from above. Seven people — three elderly men and four women — huddled around a small fire.
Healers.
They were traveling to Stonegard.
Peaceful.
Vulnerable.
The hunt began.
Zarveth slid down the slope as if he were part of the darkness itself.
The first man did not even have time to turn his head — Zarveth’s hand passed through his chest, tore out his heart, and threw it into the sand.
The second tried to stand, but before he could breathe, his skull shattered against a stone that Zarveth lifted without effort.
The third old healer attempted to flee — Zarveth caught him and snapped his neck with his fingers so easily it seemed almost gentle.
The women screamed.
But in the desert, no one hears screams.
The first of them was consumed by black flames — magic that burned everything without leaving a trace.
The second ran three steps before the sand beneath her feet cracked open, and from it burst a shadow shaped like a tentacle that dragged her beneath the ground.
The third fought.
She slashed at him with a dagger — but Zarveth slit her throat with a single motion, and blood sprayed into the air in a dark arc.
Only one remained.
A young woman with an angelic face.
Her face was pale, her eyes wide with terror. She knelt on the ground, trembling.
Zarveth studied her.
She was perfect.
He grabbed her by the arm and dragged her aside. The other bodies he left behind, half-buried in sand, half-devoured by darkness.
Zarveth knelt in the sand and began to draw a symbol with black ash:
An eye without a pupil — hollow, empty, staring into nothingness.
Around it he wrote twisted runes — warped symbols that writhed beneath his hand as if they refused to belong to this world.
The sign did not belong to the living.
It belonged to the Shadows. And they had come to claim it.
The air thickened.
The sand darkened.
The sky dimmed.
Zarveth whispered.
Words that were never meant to be heard.
A language that clawed at reality itself.
The girl was bound by shadow — tendrils curling from her wrists into the ground. Her heart pounded, but she could not escape.
She could not.
And then— a crack.
Like glass breaking under pressure. The Shadow came. From nothingness, from places without names, a stream of smoky darkness burst forth.
Formless.
Mad.
Hungry.
Zarveth guided it toward the girl.
She trembled, as if her body sensed the approaching end. Tears filled her eyes.
But tears do not help when the Shadow comes.
The stream of darkness entered her through her chest.
Her body arched.
Bones cracked.
Her hair darkened.
Her eyes turned green.
Her skin faded into a waxy pallor.
And then she stood. She was no longer herself.
From her mouth came a whisper that spread in every direction — dark, alien, deeper than her voice could ever have been.
“Glory to Morr'kalil, Lord of the Abyss. Let his chains be broken.”
Her eyes glowed.
Darkness pulsed through her veins.
Her breath reeked of decay.
Zarveth knelt.
Reverently, with his gaze lowered to the sand.
Then he lifted his head, his eyes burning with the same light.
“Glory to Morr'kalil,” he repeated with calm strength. “Let his chains be broken. Let him rise from the abyss of the world.”
The wind rose suddenly, as if the desert itself had heard their words.
A rumble in the earth echoed like a distant heartbeat — not human, but something greater.
Something still sleeping…and beginning to awaken.
Zarveth bent toward the girl, whose body was now inhabited by darkness.
He was calm, but his voice carried a command that could not be ignored.
“Adjust your vessel. Let it appear as before. Let no one see the Shadow within you.”
The girl — or what now dwelled inside her — nodded.
She closed her eyes.
A wave of magic spread beneath her skin, as if threads of darkness withdrew into the depths. The blackened strands of hair regained their golden color. Eyes that had burned with green light turned blue again.
Her skin returned to a soft, healthy tone.
Only a small trace remained.
A spark in her eyes.
A coldness that did not fade.
“Your form is now human. Their eyes will not uncover you. And their hearts… will be easy to deceive.”
The girl nodded silently.
But from her throat came a cold voice carrying the echo of another will: “I understand. I await your command.”
Zarveth stepped closer to her, his gaze darkening.
“Enter Stonegard. Be silent. Watch. Listen. Learn who is weak… and who can be broken.”
He leaned closer, his voice deepening: “We have an ally inside the castle. He does not know of you. But he will recognize the sign when the time comes. Until then — play your role. And play it well.”
A hint of a cruel smile twisted his lips.
“You are their hope. Their light. That is how they will remember you… until the moment you tear their hearts from their chests.”
The girl lowered her head.
For a brief moment, a dark spark flashed in her eyes.
Then she vanished into the sand — like a shadow that had never existed.
