Chapter XIV. - Echoes in the water
The tent was spacious and shaded by thick fabric that softened both light and heat. Inside, the air carried the scent of cinnamon and incense. The floor was covered with soft rugs, and blankets and cushions lay neatly arranged upon them.
Aaliyah slipped off her cloak with relief and sat down.
Rowena stretched and, with a sigh, collapsed onto the nearest cushion.
“Finally, some peace,” she murmured, closing her eyes. Her voice was tired but content. After days of heat and sand, she had learned to appreciate even such simple luxury.
James remained standing near the entrance, scanning the surroundings.
“Rest for a while,” he said. “I’ll find out what’s happening here — and whether anyone has heard anything.”
Aaliyah looked at him with a hint of concern, but she said nothing. She knew nothing would stop him. And just like him, she felt that something beneath the surface of the oasis was smoldering.
After he left, silence settled inside the tent, broken only by the muffled sounds of the marketplace outside.
Rowena opened one eye and spoke. “You said you like coming back here. Does that mean you grew up here?”
Aaliyah smiled faintly and ran her fingertips along the edge of the rug. “Not exactly here. I lived in a nearby city — Vienta. A beautiful, exotic place. On one side, the azure sea… and on the other, the golden desert.”
She paused for a moment, as if hearing the echo of ship bells and the rustling of curtains in the wind. “The oasis was where we came to trade… but also to find peace.”
Rowena chuckled softly. “So you came here for quiet? I don’t believe that. You usually look for adventure, not peace. Unless there’s a handsome man here named Peace.”
Aaliyah pretended to frown, then burst into laughter.
She tossed a cushion at Rowena. “I’ll show you handsome men!”
Rowena deflected the cushion lightly and shook her head, laughing. “Am I wrong? Every time we went somewhere together, men stuck to you like flies.”
Aaliyah giggled and covered her face with her hand.
“That wasn’t my fault! I just attract them. There’s nothing wrong with that.”
Rowena leaned closer, her eyes sparkling with amusement. “I never said there was.”
Suddenly, a strange sensation passed through Rowena, and her laughter stopped at once.
Aaliyah noticed the sudden change in her expression and leaned toward her.
“Did something happen?” she asked carefully.
Rowena shook her head, trying to keep her face calm. “Nothing… I just get a strange feeling sometimes.”
Aaliyah nodded quietly. “Maybe we should lie down and get some rest.”
Rowena nodded, leaned back against the cushion, and slowly closed her eyes.
The orb inside her bag flickered faintly.
While Aaliyah stared at the ceiling of the tent, their breathing gradually fell into rhythm with the distant voices of the marketplace — and soon both women drifted into sleep.

Outside the tent, daylight slowly faded into the amber glow of evening. The sun sank behind the desert horizon, and the oasis gradually grew quiet.
Meanwhile, James walked through the marketplace, his eyes searching for signs of unrest.
Harim walked a short distance ahead of him, guiding him through a maze of stalls until they reached a small stone temple hidden beneath an overhanging cliff.
From the outside, it looked unremarkable.
But when they stepped inside, they entered a chamber bathed in blue-green light.
“This is the Spring of Knowledge,” Harim said quietly. “Long ago, priests came here to seek guidance. Now only the old — and those who still believe that water has memory — visit this place.”
At the center of the room stood a small stone basin.
When James leaned over it, he saw his own reflection in the water… and then the image changed.
Instead of his eyes, another pair stared back at him — eyes without pupils.
James straightened abruptly, breathing hard.
Harim caught him as he staggered. “What did you see?”
“A warning,” James replied hoarsely.

James walked through the narrow streets of the oasis. Dusk painted the tent walls in shades of gold and crimson, but he barely noticed. His thoughts were elsewhere. The image of those pupil-less eyes kept returning to him. His chest tightened, and a cold sweat ran down his back. When he returned to the tent, night had already fallen. Lantern lights at the tops of the tents flickered in the evening breeze. He stepped inside and hesitated.
It was quiet.
Aaliyah was just waking up. She sat on the edge of a blanket, looking around as if trying to remember where she was. When she saw him, she straightened. “We have to move on,” he said without introduction. “Tomorrow morning. At first light.”
Aaliyah frowned in confusion. “Why so soon? We still haven’t figured out what to do with the orb.”
James looked at her seriously. “There’s no time for that now,” he said briefly.
“I’ll explain everything tomorrow — once we leave this place. For now, go back to sleep.”
Aaliyah frowned again. “Are you sure we shouldn’t talk about this now? Should I wake Rowena?”
“No,” James replied. “Let her sleep. We’ll all need strength for the journey — and we both know Rowena has the least of it.”
They both looked at Rowena.
She had been sleeping for some time, but the exhaustion had not yet left her face. Her skin was paler than usual, her lips cracked, and dark shadows lingered beneath her eyes.
Aaliyah took a deep breath, her gaze softening for a moment. “You’re right,” she said quietly.
“She needs rest.”
After a pause, she asked: “Where are we going?”
“To Stonegard Castle,” he answered without hesitation. “The headquarters of the Stonegard resistance is there. We must warn the command and learn more about the orbs.”
Silence followed.
James turned away from her and stared into the darkness.
Deep inside, he hoped Rowena would survive the journey to the castle — even though he strongly doubted it.
