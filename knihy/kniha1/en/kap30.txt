Chapter XXX. – In the House of light
The chambers in the eastern wing of the castle were reserved for guests who needed peace. Brienne was given one of the larger rooms — an old chamber overlooking the garden, from which the muted murmur of a fountain and the chirping of sparrows in the olive trees reached her.
While the maids helped her remove her torn clothes and washed the scratches on her legs, Brienne remained silent. She gazed out the window, quietly, almost absentmindedly.
But inside… inside, she was counting steps. Measuring distances. Listening to voices, sensing the magic in the corridors, tracking the movements of the guards.
She was given clean clothes made of fine linen, warm broth, and crisp bread. A young maid offered to assist her with a bath, but she only shook her head and lowered her gaze back to the floor.
“Thank you. I can manage on my own.”
The maid gently touched her shoulder.
“We will leave you alone for a while, my lady. If you need anything, just ring the bell.”
Brienne nodded.
And when she was alone, her eyes darkened.
She stood. Every step was light and certain. She walked to the fireplace, where flames flickered, and placed her fingers on the stone above it.
Old stone, steeped in smoke and magic.
She laid her palm upon it and whispered something in the ancient language of the Shadows.
Images poured into her mind:
Guards in the armory.
The seal storage chamber.
Commander Hander’s quarters.
The room of silver seals.
Parsons in the shrine behind the library.
The fortress breathed magic — pure, radiant, yet vulnerable.
On the southern side, the castle was protected by a high cliff, beneath which stretched the sea, while on all other sides it was surrounded by a harsh desert — and yet neither rock, nor water, nor sand could stop the darkness.
Because it was already inside.
And its name was Brienne.

The next day, Parsons came to see her.
In her hand, she held a silver amulet — simple, smooth, shaped like a teardrop.
“This belonged to Evalyne,” she said gently. “We found it near her body. It is yours.”
Brienne held her breath. Her eyes filled with tears.
She accepted the amulet with trembling fingers and then fell into Parsons’s arms. In her embrace, she trembled — so convincingly, so sincerely, that even the mage herself felt emotion welling in her eyes.
“You have a pure heart, child,” Parsons whispered.
“Not anymore,” Brienne murmured and looked away.

Two days later, she moved more freely through the castle.
In the common dining hall, an older healer grew fond of her and offered her herbal tea. A scribe brought her a book of legends when he learned she loved reading. Knights invited her to the training hall to show her how young recruits practiced.
Brienne smiled, occasionally laughed, but always quietly, humbly.
Exactly as expected.
To the others, she was like a ray of light after a storm — proof that even after a massacre, something innocent could survive.
She played her role perfectly.
