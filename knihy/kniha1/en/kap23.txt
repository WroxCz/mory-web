Chapter XXIII. - A night memory
Everyone else was asleep.
Only Rowena sat beside the dying fire, where the warmth of the embers was fading as slowly as her thoughts. The wind played quietly with the edges of her cloak while her eyes stared into the darkness.
Beyond the dunes, the silhouettes of Stonegard’s walls rose — black and sharp.
She rested her chin on her knees, and one name echoed in her mind.
Thomas.
Her breath caught.
How long had it been since she had seen him? Months. Too many months.
And now… now they would finally meet again.
The thought warmed her chest and tightened her stomach at the same time. She was excited — and just as afraid. Joy mixed with uncertainty that slipped beneath her skin like cold.
What if his feelings had changed? What if hers were the only ones that had endured?
Their relationship had never been simple.
She — a healer of the Tower of Pure Light, bound by an oath to care only for the well-being of others, never her own.
And he… a knight for whom family was a burden. A weakness an enemy could exploit.
And yet, they had found each other.
Despite orders.
Despite oaths.

She remembered their first meeting.
It had been in the Tower of Pure Light, in the infirmary bathed in the soft glow of lamps and filled with the scent of herbs. They had brought in an entire unit after a raid in the Abyss of Syleus.
Rowena had been kneeling beside a wounded man, her hands stained with blood mixed with healing salve, her mind focused only on her work.
She did not lift her head until she heard the voice of the head healer above her.
“Althea will take over,” she said briefly. “You come with me. We have severe cases in the northern wing.”
Rowena stepped aside without hesitation, quickly wiping her hands on a cloth and following her into another part of the infirmary.
There, there was no calm.
The air was heavier, filled with pain, muffled cries, and the metallic smell of blood.
And that was where she saw him.
He stood nearby — tall and broad in scratched armor. His dark red hair was tied into a braid, the sides of his head shaved. Tension reflected in his brown eyes.
In his arms, he carried another knight.
Gravely wounded.
The man hung limp, his head falling helplessly to his chest, blood running down his armor and dripping onto the stone floor.
The knight held him firmly — but carefully — as if afraid that a single wrong movement might hurt him even more.
At that moment, their eyes met.
He froze.
Only slightly.
But Rowena noticed.
His breath caught for a fraction of a second.
“Please,” he said quietly, without taking his eyes off her. “Help him.”
Rowena nodded before she even realized it.
“Lay him here,” she said, pointing to an empty bed. Her voice was calm, steady — the voice of someone who knew what she was doing.
The knight obeyed immediately. He knelt and carefully placed the wounded man on the bed.
As he released his grip, his hands trembled for a moment.
Rowena noticed that.
Just as she noticed the blood on his forearm, the small cuts on his knuckles, the tear in his glove.
“You’re injured,” she said quietly. “You should have yourself treated as well.”
The knight glanced briefly at his hand, then lifted his eyes to hers again.
“It’s nothing,” he replied calmly. “Just scratches.”
Rowena inhaled, then silently bent over the wounded man.
She loosened the blood-soaked straps, pushed aside the remnants of armor, and began to work.
Her fingers touched the injured skin lightly, and a gentle pearlescent light spread between them.
The wounds began to close beneath her touch, and the bleeding slowed.
Thomas remained standing nearby.
He did not speak.
He simply watched.
When the wounded man gasped sharply and jerked in pain, Thomas stepped forward without being asked and placed his hand on the man’s shoulder.
Firmly.
That touch was not only support for the wounded — it felt as if it held the entire moment together.
It struck Rowena more deeply than she expected.
The pearlescent light between her fingers flickered.
Her magic reacted to her unease, intensifying, losing its smoothness.
She took a quick breath and forced herself to focus.
Still, she lifted her eyes. Their gazes met. There was a calmness in his that disarmed her.
She looked away and finished the healing with one final silent sweep of light.
When she withdrew her hands, she realized her fingers were trembling slightly.
That startled her more than anything else.
It had never happened before.
She straightened. Her heart beat faster, and the sensation unsettled her.
She took a slow breath, forcing herself to find the balance she expected of herself.
“He’ll be fine,” she said quietly, stepping back.
The words were certain, practiced, safe. Words she could hide behind. She was already turning away, ready to leave quickly.
And then she felt a touch.
His hand closed gently around her wrist.
Not roughly.
Not violently.
Firmly — yet gently.
The warmth of it shot up her arm, stealing her breath for a moment.
“Perhaps,” Thomas’s voice said softly, closer than she expected, “my injuries might need treatment after all.”
She froze, but did not pull away.
She simply turned her head slowly to look at him.
“I’ll call someone,” she replied quickly — perhaps too quickly.
She tried to withdraw her hand, but he did not release her.
Instead, he shook his head.
Calmly.
Decisively.
“No,” he said quietly. “I want you to do it.”
She hesitated.
“Alright,” she said at last.
The word came out softer than she intended.
Her gaze slid from his face down to the hand still holding hers — then to the cracks in his armor, the dried blood, the cuts she had overlooked before.
They were not serious. She knew they were merely an excuse.
Gently, she freed herself from his grasp — this time without resistance — and stepped closer.
Too close.
She had to take another breath before daring to raise her hands.
“Stand still,” she said matter-of-factly, as if that could restore order.
When her fingers touched his skin, the magic flowed again — quieter than before, more cautious.
The light clung close to her palms, as if it too hesitated.
Rowena focused on the wounds, on their edges, on the rhythm of her breathing. When the last cut closed and the pearlescent light faded, she lowered her hands. Her magic settled within her, dissolving into familiar silence.
And in that moment, his fingers closed around her hand again. This time differently. He slid her palm between his hands — slowly, carefully.
His palms were warm.
Rough.
Real.
She lifted her eyes.
He was looking at her. Directly. There was something gentle in his gaze.
She did not pull away and did not step back.
He noticed that stillness — that quiet consent.
He slowly released one hand from hers, as if giving her time to retreat if she wished, and guided the other upward.
When his fingers touched her face, the contact was barely there.
Warmth spread across her skin, and Rowena felt as if the entire world had stopped.
His touch was firm yet tender — completely unlike the rough hand of a warrior she might have expected. His thumb rested against her cheekbone.
For a moment, it remained still.
Then it moved.
Lightly. Slowly.
His thumb brushed gently across her cheek — a faint motion without urgency or claim.
He lingered in that slow, almost invisible movement, as if committing the moment to memory.
Then his thumb stilled, and he leaned slightly closer — just enough for her to hear him.
“You are beautiful,” he said softly.
The words struck her. For a moment, she could not respond.
She simply stood there, his hand on her face, her breath caught somewhere between inhale and exhale.
Her hand moved slightly and instinctively touched his wrist.
Not to push him away. But to make sure he was real.
“You shouldn’t say things like that,” she whispered. Her voice was barely audible — tense and fragile at once.
Her fingers still rested lightly on his wrist, as if that touch were the only thing anchoring her to the present.
For a second, nothing happened.
Then something shifted in his eyes.
“I know,” he answered quietly. “But I can’t help it.”
She knew she should step back.
At the same time, she realized how difficult that would be.
And then footsteps sounded.
Reality pierced through her like a sudden sting.
The infirmary door opened, and another healer entered, lost in her own thoughts, carrying a bowl of clean bandages. She did not look at them and walked straight toward the opposite table.
Rowena’s heart pounded in her throat.
Thomas was still too close. His hand had not yet fully left her face.
Their posture was too intimate — too different from what should be seen.
Slowly, almost imperceptibly, she stepped away. Her fingers slipped from his wrist, as if nothing had happened.
“The wounds are healed,” she said — this time firmly.
Professional.
Steady.
Thomas inhaled. Once. Deeply.
Then he stepped closer again — only a small step — and leaned toward her so that his voice was barely audible.
“Come tonight,” he whispered calmly. “To the forest by the lake. After dark.”
His gaze was open.
Without pressure.
“If you don’t want to, I’ll understand. I won’t come looking for you. I won’t tell anyone.”
He stepped back to give her space.
“Only…” he added quietly, “I would like to speak with you. Without witnesses.”

From that moment on, they met in secret — between duties, beyond the walls of the tower and the fortress.
Then came the order. Thomas had to return to Stonegard Castle.
Before they parted, they promised to write to each other. And they kept that promise. The letters truly came.
At first glance, they seemed innocent — simple mentions of weather, duty, and ordinary days.
The words were chosen carefully, so they revealed nothing more than allowed.
But between the lines — between the words about weather and service — there was something else.
Tenderness. Memories. And a bond that distance could not break.

Morning was cold and pale.
The desert wind had weakened, but in its wake remained a raw silence — the kind that comes just before dawn.
The fire in the camp had long burned out. The embers were covered in a layer of gray ash, and the air smelled of dust, sweat, and something intangible.
Rowena still sat in the same place, knees drawn to her chest, her head resting against her shoulder.
She had not slept.
Her eyes were closed, but her mind remained awake — half in the distance, half in memory.
When she finally opened her eyes, a pink line of the new day already smoldered along the horizon.
The walls of Stonegard, which had seemed like a dream barrier in the night, were now real.
Stone.
Unbreakable.
Footsteps on gravel pulled her from her thoughts.
“Did you sleep at all?” a quiet voice asked.
James.
She turned her head toward him and smiled — tired, but grateful. “There was no need. Sometimes it’s enough just to sit.”
He sat beside her.
He brought a cup of warm drink and handed it to her without a word. It was a simple mixture of herbs — bitter, but comforting.
Rowena took a sip and let the warmth spread through her stomach.
“You’re pale,” he said quietly.
“It’s nothing.”
They sat in silence.
James was not the kind of man who forced words — and she appreciated that.
When Aaliyah rose, the camp was already nearly dismantled. The knights gathered weapons, saddled horses, extinguished the last traces of heat.
DaVinci stood at the edge of the camp, thoughtful, his eyes fixed on the horizon. “Today we reach Stonegard,” he said as the group assembled around him. “If nothing stops us along the way, we’ll arrive shortly after noon. Prepare yourselves.”
The sun slowly rose above the horizon.
Gold spilled across shields, helmets, and the edges of cloaks.
The sand shimmered, and in the distance the stone walls of the fortress wavered like a mirage.
And yet…they were real.
Every step the horses took toward them felt like a heartbeat.
Stronger.
Faster.
It was him. He was close.
Stonegard Castle was drawing near.
