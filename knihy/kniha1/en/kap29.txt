Chapter XXIX. – Before the Gates of light
The gates of Stonegard Castle opened with the heavy groan of iron. The castle courtyard was calm, yet attentive — with every newcomer, a story entered these walls, and in these days, stories had become painfully frequent.
Yet the arrival of the girl drew everyone’s attention.
Barefoot, her dress coated in sand and dust. Golden hair tangled, streaked in places with dried blood. Her face was pale, nearly colorless, with a thin bruise at her temple.
And her eyes.
They were absent. Unfocused. As if they were still staring at the moment when the Shadow had slaughtered people she knew, and her mind could not tear itself away from it.
The courtyard fell silent.
In the midst of that silence stood DaVinci, Hander, and Parsons. All three remained quiet as she came to stand before them.
“She calls herself Brienne,” one of the knights said. “We found her in the desert. Her caravan was attacked, and she was the only survivor. She claims they were attacked by a Shadow.”
DaVinci briefly turned his gaze toward Hander.
Hander caught the look — and frowned. His jaw tightened; he was already refusing to admit where those words might lead.
“Brienne,” Parsons addressed her gently, “you are safe here. Can you tell us what happened?”
The girl trembled slightly. Her lips quivered. One of the knights placed a hand on her back and gestured for her to step forward. Brienne shrank inward, but obeyed.
At first, she did not answer.
She swallowed heavily, as if her tongue would not obey her. Then she nodded, and her voice sounded like a spiderweb torn by the wind:
“There were seven of us. Healers. My teacher’s name was Evalyne. She raised me… she was like a mother to me. In the evening, on our way to Stonegard, we made camp. We wanted to help the local healers.”
She fell silent. Her hands trembled.
“In the night… it came. Like darkness without a face. Like death. A creature from the desert, without eyes, without a soul. It burned one of us before we could even breathe. The others… I heard their screams. His hands… his voice…”
She broke into tears. Not dramatically, but quietly, painfully. Parsons stepped closer and handed her a handkerchief, which Brienne pressed to her eyes.
“I ran. I ran because I was a coward.” She looked up, her eyes full of tears.
“But when I escaped… I begged the Light to destroy me. Because why me? Why did I survive… and not someone better?”
Silence settled over the courtyard.
Hander studied her carefully, his fingers — which usually remained still — tightening slightly, as if he felt the girl’s grief deep in his bones.
Parsons nodded slowly. Her voice was quiet.
“Stonegard welcomes you, Brienne. Your loss grieves us deeply. And your testimony may save lives. You will rest, under supervision. When the knights who went to verify your story return, we will speak again.”
Brienne nodded.
“Thank you. Thank you… for believing me.”
Parsons watched her for a moment, then stepped aside and quietly instructed one of the mages:
“Take care of her. Give her food and clean clothes. Let no one near her until she recovers.”
As they led her away, their eyes followed her. Guards whispered among themselves about what the massacre must have been like. About how young she was. About the terrible fate that had befallen her.
One of the squires even wiped away a tear.
No one noticed that in Brienne’s eyes, lowered toward the ground, something flickered for the briefest moment.
Something deeper.
Something cold.
And high upon the stone ledge of one of the ramparts, hidden within a magical illusion, stood Zarveth.
Silent.
Unseen.
He watched everything. Every gesture. Every glance. His face slowly twisted into a satisfied smirk.
Perfect, he thought. More perfect than I expected.
And the castle opened its gates not only to a girl.
It opened them to the Shadows.
