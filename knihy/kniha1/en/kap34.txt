Chapter XXXIV – The Bells

The rays of sunlight pushed only timidly through the stone shutters. Silence filled the room, broken only by slow, muffled breathing. Thomas lay on his stomach, his face buried in a soft pillow.

But then it came.

From the distance, bells rang out. Sharp. Urgent. Not celebratory.
It was not a greeting to a new day.
It was a warning.

He sat up immediately.
“Alarm?”

Without hesitation, Thomas rose, his expression changing in an instant from sleepy to alert and stern. He quickly pulled on his trousers, put on his shirt, and, sword in hand, ran into the corridor. His footsteps thundered across the stone floor as he hurried toward the courtyard.

There, activity was already underway.

A group of knights stood gathered by the stone fountain, among them William, leaning against the railing.

“You heard it too?” Thomas asked, slightly out of breath.

“Hard to miss,” William replied, glancing toward the gate, where more knights, squires, and several mages were already assembling. At the center stood a tall man in armor — Commander Hander.

As soon as he noticed the two men approaching, he frowned and motioned for them to join.

“During the night, the security of the castle was breached,” he announced clearly.
“One of the patrols moving through the lower levels noticed that a set of doors leading into the underground passages had been left partially open.”

“Someone forgot to close them?” Thomas asked.

Hander nodded.

“The captain of the patrol sent two knights to check the area. A few corridors farther, in a dead-end passage beneath the northern tower, they found a body. A young boy. Something tore him to pieces.”

“Do we know his identity?” William asked calmly, though something shifted beneath his composed exterior.

“Yes,” Hander said darkly.
“His name was Avel. A kitchen assistant. A dish washer. He had worked here for several months — quiet, hardworking, without trouble.”

“Did anyone see him last night?” Thomas asked, his brow tightening.

“The last person to see him was another kitchen helper before leaving. Avel stayed longer, apparently because he still had dishes to finish. Since then, no one has seen him.”
Hander hesitated.

“No one heard any screams?” Thomas raised an eyebrow.

“No. Nothing. No noise, no magical disturbance. He simply… vanished and died.”

William looked down at the paving stones, appearing thoughtful while, inside, the pieces of the puzzle began to fall into place.

Brienne had chosen her victim wisely.
A quiet, solitary boy. Unnoticed. Perfect for a ritual.
And no one had seen a thing.

“Sir, we should seal access to the underground and question everyone who was on duty last night as soon as possible,” he suggested aloud.

“We have already begun,” Hander nodded.
“But so far, nothing. No testimony. No traces.”

Thomas tightened his grip on the hilt of his sword.

“This was no accident. Someone led that boy down there. Why else would he go?”

“I suggest that I personally take charge of the investigation. I know the corridors. I know the staff. I can speak with the cooks, the guards, the servants. Perhaps someone noticed a small detail we have overlooked.”

Hander nodded, grateful for the initiative.

“I welcome it, Knight William. We must keep this quiet. Panic among the people would only complicate matters. I entrust this matter to you. I trust you will not disappoint me.”

William bowed slightly.

“Thank you, sir.”

Hander said nothing more. He turned and strode away decisively.

Thomas nodded, his trust absolute.

“I’ll help you, if you want. Together we might find more.”

William looked at him.

“I appreciate that, Thomas,” he said quietly with a faint smirk.
“But… you should be with Rowena. You two do not have much time. No one knows when she may have to leave again. Make the most of those moments. So you don’t spend the next few months whining to me.”

“All right,” Thomas agreed, stepping back.
“I’ll be with her. But if you need anything…”

“I know where to find you,” William replied softly.

As Thomas turned back toward the castle to return to his room and prepare for training, William slowly headed toward the kitchen. His steps were calm, but his thoughts churned wildly.

Brienne is already acting.
Too soon. Too openly.

In the corners of his mind, threads of lies began to weave themselves — subtle distortions of events, hints of false trails that would lead suspicion elsewhere. He had to protect her — not as an ally, but as a threat. If anyone came too close to the truth, her failure could drag him down with her.

And he was not prepared to die for someone else’s recklessness.

He had to keep her under control.

His step was quieter than the whisper of wind, and in every movement lay the skill and experience of a hunter. Under the pretense of investigating the open door discovered by the patrol during the night, he reached the place where the boy’s body had been found — the death that had shaken the stone walls of Stonegard.

As he approached, his eyes caught something the others had overlooked.

On the dark stone floor, barely visible, lay a symbol drawn in black ash — an eye without a lens, surrounded by a tangle of ancient runes. A mark that carried the weight of dark power and reminded him of the commands he had received from the depths.

This symbol was a key — one that could reveal more than William wished.

Slowly, he pulled a small vial of magical powder from his cloak. Carefully, he sprinkled it over the spot, masking every trace of magic. Then he covered the area with small stones, arranging them precisely so that nothing would appear suspicious.

He focused on one thing alone — leaving no trace behind.

Afterward, he took from his pocket a small amulet designed to detect dark magic. He held it in his palm for a moment, waiting for a tremor, for cold, for any reaction.

Nothing.

The amulet did not respond.

The place was clean.

Everything was exactly as it should be.

William allowed himself a faint smile. Only then did he slowly straighten.

It was time to continue playing his game.

With icy calm, he returned to the courtyard. His face was composed, betraying no hint of what he truly knew or planned. Among the knights and mages, he appeared to be one of them — an ally who could be trusted.

But in truth, he stood somewhere else entirely — caught in the trap of his own ambitions and treacherous plans, slowly weaving them together to bring darkness and chaos exactly as he desired.
