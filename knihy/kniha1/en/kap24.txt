Chapter XXIV. - Between the walls
The stone gate of Stonegard slammed shut behind them with a heavy thud.
James, Aaliyah, Rowena, and several knights arrived at the main courtyard. Sunlight broke against the tower roofs, casting sharp shadows across the walls, and the stone paving beneath their feet echoed under the weight of horses, boots, and armor.
Others were already waiting.
The knights of Stonegard stood in perfectly aligned rows, steady and disciplined — yet when their gazes shifted toward the trio, the silence changed ever so slightly. All eyes turned toward the gate, tension settling into their shoulders.
One glance was enough.
Everyone understood that something had happened.
Something very bad.
DaVinci’s firm voice rang across the courtyard.
“I and this trio will report immediately to the commander of the knights,” he announced, marking them with a brief gesture. “And to the High Mage.”
The surrounding bustle turned into a muted hum — barking orders, clattering weapons, pounding boots, fragments of hurried speech.
But Rowena barely noticed.
She was not looking at DaVinci.
She was searching for a face she had missed more than anything.
And then she saw it.
He stood at the right edge of the formation, among the others — and yet strangely alone.
Thomas held his spear firmly in his right hand, its tip pointing toward the sky. A long sword swayed at his side, half hidden in the deep shadow. His posture was straight, quiet, ready — as if he himself were part of the stone wall that had decided to breathe.
When he noticed Rowena, surprise flickered across his face — quickly suppressed.
Rowena moved.
She stepped into place beside James and Aaliyah and, without a single word, followed DaVinci.
Behind her, she felt Thomas’s gaze.

Evening fell, and the air slowly cooled.
The shadows of the gate stretched into the sand beyond the walls, and the dunes in the distance rolled gently in the quiet movement of the wind.
Thomas stood on watch, motionless, his eyes fixed on the desert — on the sand and the land beyond the gate that was preparing for sleep.
Then something unfamiliar appeared in that stillness.
Movement.
Something that did not belong to the calm of evening — fast, purposeful. It was approaching. Thomas instinctively reached for his spyglass and raised it to his eyes.
The image sharpened.
Uncertain shapes became clear forms: two knights on horseback, riding hard straight toward the gate.
Blue cloaks streamed behind them in the wind.
He recognized them instantly.
Knights serving under Commander DaVinci.
Thomas froze.
He kept the spyglass to his eyes for another moment, as if hoping he was mistaken.
He was not.
If they were returning this fast, without stopping, it meant only one thing —
Something had gone wrong.
And not slightly.
He lowered the spyglass and wasted no time.
Turning sharply, he strode from the wall, his pace quickly becoming a run. Heavy boots thundered against stone, his cloak snapping behind him.
He climbed the narrow stairs into the watchtower, where his friend was stationed.
William sat at a small table beneath a narrow window, leaning against the wall. His rune-marked shield rested nearby.
He lifted his head the moment he heard the footsteps.
“What’s happening?” he asked calmly, though readiness already showed in his eyes.
Thomas stopped only long enough to catch his breath.
“Two knights. Blue cloaks. Riding straight here,” he said briefly.
William rose without another question.
He grabbed his shield, slung it over his shoulder, and headed for the stairs. Thomas followed immediately behind.
They descended the walls two steps at a time. The stone beneath their boots was slick, but neither slowed. Guards turned to watch them, instinctively sensing urgency and clearing the path.
They reached the gate moments before the riders.
Thomas took his position, spear firm in his hand. William stood beside him with shield and sword ready.
Both fixed their gaze on the dunes.
The riders were close now.
With every thunder of hooves, it became clearer — the time of calm was over.
When they reached the gate, they leapt from their saddles. Dust swirled around them.
“We are messengers sent by Commander DaVinci,” the first said, breathing hard. “His group rides behind us. Slower.”
William’s gaze darkened. “Why slower?”
The second knight removed his helmet.
“They are escorting civilians. They were attacked… by something evil. Not bandits.” He hesitated briefly. “We also encountered undead. And some kind of dark ritual in the ruins near the eastern dunes.”
Silence at the gate grew heavy.
DaVinci was not a man who took unnecessary risks.
He was a seasoned commander, known for acting with precision and force when needed. If he had chosen to take civilians with him and return, it meant the situation was serious — perhaps worse than anyone wanted to admit.
“The ritual was active,” the first knight added quietly. “Dark symbols in the sand. Blood. But… it didn’t appear complete.”
Thomas and William exchanged grim looks.
William immediately took command.
“Take the horses to the stables. Then both of you to the castle kitchens — food, water. Recover yourselves. After that, you will give a full report.”
The knights nodded and disappeared into the depths of the fortress.
William turned to Thomas.
“Return to your post. The moment Commander DaVinci arrives, I want to know immediately. I’m going to Commander Hander and High Mage Parsons.”
Thomas nodded and, without another word, returned to the walls, his gaze once again fixed on the dunes.
William strode through the corridors toward the commander’s hall.
If Commander DaVinci had intervened in a dark ritual, it meant only one thing. This was not just about rescuing civilians.
Something greater had awakened.



Within minutes, orders echoed through the castle corridors.
Guards moved into position, gates were secured, reinforcements summoned from the inner courtyards. Kitchens began preparing food and hot water, infirmaries opened.
Stonegard was awakening into a state it knew all too well.
Thomas returned to his post.
He stood at the gate, spear resting against his shoulder, eyes fixed on the dunes — waiting.
Footsteps behind him warned him before anyone spoke.
William had returned and took his place beside him.
“Commander Hander and High Mage Parsons have been informed,” he said quietly, without ceremony. “The castle is on alert.”
Thomas gave a slight nod, never taking his eyes off the horizon.
“Now we wait,” William continued. “Until Commander DaVinci arrives with his group. And those civilians.”
There was no nervousness in his voice.
Only heavy certainty.
Thomas tightened his grip on the spear.

The waiting stretched into the next day.
The sun climbed high above the walls, and the morning chill gave way to sharp, relentless heat. The sand beyond the gate blazed almost white, and the dunes shimmered in the wavering haze.
Thomas remained at his post.
Guards rotated shifts, orders passed quietly, but he stayed.
His gaze fixed on the horizon, as if sheer will could make the silhouettes appear sooner.
Meanwhile, William moved repeatedly between the walls and the courtyard. He checked the guard positions, spoke briefly with the mages, ensured the infirmary and healers were ready.
Everything ran exactly as it should.
And still, time dragged.
Every movement on the dunes sparked hope — only to have the wind scatter it.
Every distant shadow became a rider for a moment…and then only a trick of the heated air.
The tension slowly thickened.
No longer a sharp grip — but a steady pressure that could not be shaken off.
Then, shortly after noon, Thomas narrowed his eyes.
At last, a true silhouette appeared on the horizon.
And behind it — another.
The shadows multiplied, separating from one another, taking shape.
No longer a mirage.
No longer a trick of light.
They were real.
Riders.
People.
Thomas straightened and raised his hand in the prearranged signal.
The guards on the walls reacted instantly. Voices carried quietly across the stone, orders passed without shouting.
The figures approached slowly.
It was them.
DaVinci and his group.

The gate of Stonegard Castle began to prepare to open.
Thomas and William moved at once.
Without unnecessary words, they descended from the battlements down to the gate, their steps firm and certain, weapons ready. Spears and swords were at hand, shields within reach. Not because they expected an attack — but because experience had taught them to be prepared.
They took positions at either side of the gate, their stances clearly saying: if anything else emerged from the sands, the fortress would answer immediately.
Thomas stood facing the gate, his gaze sharp, never ceasing to scan the surroundings. William cast a brief glance around, checked the placement of the guards, and gave a short nod.
The gate opened, and the group rode inside onto the main courtyard. The moment they were behind the walls, the guards shut it again with a heavy crash.
It was clear there were three civilians. Most likely two women and one man. The riders dismounted.
The horses were drenched in sweat, and the civilians could barely remain standing.
Thomas stepped forward and studied the man first.
His posture was solid, his movements steady despite exhaustion. By all appearances, a fighter — but not a knight. His armor was light and bore no insignia. Thomas’s gaze dropped to his weapons.
A spear. And at his belt, a pair of daggers.
His jaw tightened.
Not a knight. Not a sworn soldier. More likely a hired blade. Perhaps worse — an assassin for coin. A man without a crest and without honor, who fought and killed for silver.
Then his gaze shifted to the woman in red.
One look at her appearance — the way she carried herself, the cut of her garments, the faint trace of magic pulsing almost invisibly around her — was enough.
A fire mage from Vienta. That much was obvious.
And finally…the second woman.
Thomas drew in a breath — and his heart missed a beat. He recognized her.
Rowena.
Her face was pale, dark circles beneath her eyes. She could barely stand. Every breath looked as if it cost her more strength than she possessed.
The sight terrified him.
He wanted to run to her. To cross the space between them, take her into his arms, and shield her from the stares, from the weight of the day, from everything.
Then he noticed the mercenary lightly supporting her, keeping her from collapsing. A firm hand at her elbow.
Red flashed before Thomas’s eyes.
The whole world narrowed to that hand on her body. To the touch that did not belong there.
To the fact that she had to lean on him.
Anger surged inside him. Heavy. Burning. The kind that demanded action.
The impulse was sharp. Nearly unstoppable.
But the training of a knight was stronger.
He remained where he stood. Straight. Motionless. Hands gripping the spear tightly.
Duty came before feeling — it always had.
Yet his gaze hardened. There was no longer only fear in it.
There was decision.
He would learn what had happened — down to the smallest detail. What had driven her here, in this condition… and who that man was who dared touch her.
When the time came — and it would come — he would settle it with him as a knight of Stonegard.
A man with honor and oath.
Not as a jealous man in anger.

Rowena lifted her head and slowly looked around, as if searching for someone.
Then their eyes met.
In her gaze was a tenderness that calmed him, if only a little.
She drew a quiet breath, looked away, and without a single word followed Commander DaVinci.
Thomas watched her until she disappeared from sight.
No one around noticed anything unusual.
No one — except one man.
William.
He stood motionless, leaning against a pillar, one hand resting on the hilt of his sword, shield on his back, as if he simply belonged there.
He had been in Stonegard only two years — promoted and assigned here as Thomas’s superior. In that time, they had become the closest of friends. They were bound by far more than service — they had fought side by side in battles, trained to exhaustion, laughed in the hardest moments, and drunk together into silence in a nearby tavern.
William knew Thomas better than anyone.
“So… you found her again.” He smirked.
Serious relationships were not his way, but he wished his friend happiness — even if it was not the kind he would choose for himself.
His gaze drifted back to Rowena as she disappeared among the ranks of knights. Then back to Thomas.
At that moment, he needed to say nothing more. Not today.
But later — when they were alone, perhaps in the tower during watch, or at an old table with a mug and supper — maybe then he would remind him.
With a smile. With the calm of someone who knows when to speak… and when to stay silent.
Then his attention shifted to the other visitors.
James and Aaliyah.
Seeing them together irritated him.
Aaliyah had been nothing more than a brief acquaintance to him — a distraction he had never taken seriously. And he certainly had no desire for her to make a scene now.
As for James, whom he had known for four years, he had never liked him.
He considered him a fool, obsessed with Aaliyah, yet too cowardly to admit it. And now they traveled together.
If there was one thing William truly disliked, it was when someone took his “toys” — even the ones he himself had thrown away.
With a tense expression, he studied the group once more, then turned his attention back to Thomas.
Today was Thomas’s day.
And because of that, William let it be.

DaVinci strode through the castle corridors faster than usual.
His footsteps echoed across the stone floor, and the trio behind him struggled to keep pace.
No one spoke.
There was no time.
Every second felt too long.
When the heavy doors of the great hall appeared before them, DaVinci did not slow. He pushed them open, and they swung wide with a loud groan.
Inside, the commander of the knights, Hander, was already waiting — and beside him stood the High Mage, Parsons.
They sat at a large round table of dark oak, its surface covered with maps, seals, and unfurled scrolls. Light streaming through the tall castle windows struck the metal of Hander’s armor and cast sharp shadows across his stern face.
Both were frowning.
Hander leaned forward, elbows on the table, hands clasped before his mouth. When DaVinci entered with the trio, his gaze lifted — heavy, assessing, uncompromising.
Parsons sat upright, fingers resting lightly on the edge of a map. Her expression was calmer, but her eyes betrayed tension.
The silence lasted only a few seconds.
Then Hander slowly lowered his hands onto the table, and his voice filled the hall — deep and hard as the stone of the fortress walls.
“So what happened?” he said bluntly. “You were supposed to be on your way back to Castleton.”
He leaned forward slightly, his gaze boring into DaVinci.
“Not turning around halfway and racing back here,” he continued coldly, “as if your life depended on it.”
He paused for a moment.
“And who are these three?” he asked sharply. “I see no colors of any order on them… and they certainly don’t look like random travelers.”
Parsons finally lifted her hand from the map. Her gaze was focused, searching.
“You brought them with you for a reason,” she said calmly. “So explain it to us.”
All eyes turned back to DaVinci.
DaVinci drew a breath. For a brief moment he hesitated, then lifted his head, his voice steady despite the tension within it.
“This morning, a hawk flew into my camp,” he said. “From Harim. from the Sanctuary Oasis.”
At the name, Parsons straightened slightly, and Hander’s lips tightened into a thin line.
“It spoke of a brutal killing,” DaVinci continued. “Not an attack. A ritual murder.”
Only his voice could be heard in the hall.
“One of the young guards,” he added more quietly, “changed. Something consumed him and turned him into a dark knight.”
Hander’s hands clenched on the edge of the table. The wood creaked softly.
“Harim wrote something else in the letter,” DaVinci continued. “That dark knight is searching for three travelers — and he asked me to help them on their journey, if I found them.”
He briefly glanced over his shoulder at the trio standing behind him.
“I therefore left my camp with my company and set out to find them.”
He looked again at Hander and Parsons.
“We found them before it was too late,” he said. A shadow of anger entered his voice.
“That dark knight — Zarveth — reached them shortly before we did. He nearly killed them. But they managed to wound him. And when he was distracted by my company, they were able to drive him back.”
He paused for a moment. Then continued, more slowly.
“On our return, we encountered a dark ritual,” he said quietly. “Zarveth was performing it himself. Judging by what the undead were wearing, several knights of Stonegard were sacrificed.”
He drew a steady breath.
“That is why I returned,” DaVinci concluded. “Because this is not just a problem of the Sanctuary Oasis. It is a threat to the entire world.”
Parsons went pale.
Hander lifted his head. Suppressed anger flared in his eyes. For a moment he said nothing. Then he planted his palms on the table, and his voice came out deeper than before — rough and heavy.
“We sent a patrol into the desert,” he said slowly. “To the watchtower along the old trade road.”
His jaw tightened. “They never returned. No message. No signal.”
He raised his gaze to DaVinci.
“So now we know what happened to them,” he added quietly. “They were killed by a dark mage.”
His hands slowly curled into fists. He did not slam the table. He did not shout.
That made the calm radiating from him all the more terrifying.
But this time, DaVinci did not look away.
“No,” he said calmly.
That single word struck the hall harder than a shout.
Hander narrowed his eyes.
“That was not a dark mage,” DaVinci continued. “Not a man. Not a fanatic playing with rituals.”
A brief pause.
“It was an ancient Shadow,” he said firmly. “Something born from darkness older than our orders, our oaths… even our walls.”
Parsons raised an eyebrow but remained silent.
Hander straightened.
“I saw what it left behind,” DaVinci continued. “That was not magic a human can wield.”
Parsons finally spoke.
“I have seen rituals that were supposed to be impossible,” she said calmly. “And there was always someone alive behind them.”
Hander nodded slowly and exhaled through his nose. Then he gave a short, dry laugh.
“A dark knight. Zarveth,” he repeated. “Interesting name,” he added with irony. “Dark enough, grand enough… exactly the kind that whispers well in taverns.”
He straightened, and his gaze darkened — not with anger, but with hard skepticism.
His eyes fixed on DaVinci.
“I believe you saw something,” he continued. “I believe our men are dead. But the existence of some ancient Shadow in armor?”
He shook his head. “Nonsense.” Hander leaned back in his chair.
“An enemy of flesh and blood makes sense,” he said firmly. “A fanatic. A black mage. A fallen knight. All of that is possible. But a Shadow?”
He tilted his head slightly.
“To be honest, DaVinci,” he added, “did you see this monster… or just a man in armor?”
DaVinci did not move. When he spoke again, his voice was calm, without defensiveness.
“No,” he admitted. “I did not see him up close.”
He lifted his eyes to Hander.
“Before I could reach him, he vanished. Dissolved into the shadows as if he had never been there.” A short, dry laugh echoed through the hall. Hander shook his head.
“There we have it,” he said with a hint of mockery. “He vanished. Dissolved.”
He settled more comfortably into his chair.
“This is no monster,” he continued. “This is a dark mage. Experienced, cautious, perhaps fanatical. Men like that disappear quickly when the situation turns against them.”
Parsons slowly raised her head. She exchanged a brief glance with Hander — a silent agreement without words.
“I agree,” she said at last, calmly. “What you describe, DaVinci,” she continued, “matches a highly skilled dark mage. Someone capable of working with illusion, shadow, and ritual.”
Her voice was steady, rational.
“A Shadow would be more convenient,” she added evenly. “But the real enemy is always a human.”
Hander nodded.
“And a human can be found,” he said. “Surrounded. Killed.”
Aaliyah stepped forward sharply. Fire blazed in her eyes.
“You are wrong,” she burst out. “This was not—”
James was faster.
He caught her wrist and gave the faintest shake of his head.
Aaliyah drew a breath, teeth clenched, her gaze locked on Hander. The words she wanted to unleash remained inside her like burning ash.
Hander did not miss the movement. His gaze shifted from Aaliyah to James. This time, longer. Studying. Cold.
“Do you know what I see?” he said slowly.
He leaned forward, elbows on the table. “I see people who are exhausted to the point of death,” he continued calmly.
His voice was firm, without anger.
“And a tired mind,” he added, “is easily deceived.”
He lifted his chin.
“A dark mage frightened you. Played with shadows, rituals, and your fear,” he said. A brief pause. “Rest,” he added coldly. “Fear often turns out to be nothing more than illusion.”
Aaliyah froze.
Blood rushed to her face until it burned red with anger. Her hands trembled. The fire in her eyes was no longer just rage — it was a deep sense of injustice.
James frowned. His jaw tightened, his gaze hard, but he remained silent. He knew any words now would only make things worse.
Rowena stood motionless.
Shocked.
Her eyes widened — for the first time fully realizing that what they had seen… was something no one here wanted to hear about.
Parsons nodded.
“I agree with the commander,” she said calmly. “Fatigue and fear are the worst advisors.”
Her voice was balanced, almost soothing.
DaVinci remained silent.
He stood aside, his gaze fixed on the pair. Not because he did not know what to say — but because he understood that in this room, the decision had already been made.
And with it — the first mistake.
Hander straightened. He placed his palms flat on the table and slowly rose from his chair. His figure cast a long shadow across the maps and stone floor.
“Whoever it is,” he said firmly, “a dark mage, fanatic, or fallen knight…” He paused briefly. “…he will pay for the deaths of my knights.”
His voice was calm.
All the more terrifying because of it.
“I will not allow the spilled blood of a knight of Stonegard to go unanswered,” he continued. “I will send trackers. We will seal the desert roads. Anyone moving in the shadows will be questioned.”
His gaze swept across the trio.
“We will find him,” he added coldly. “And we will punish him.”
Parsons nodded.
“Rituals can be traced,” she said matter-of-factly. “Magic always leaves a mark.”
Hander turned toward the door, as if the matter were settled.
“This is our concern,” he concluded. “Stonegard will handle it.”
Parsons straightened the parchments before her. Her movements were calm, precise, as if her thoughts were already several steps ahead.
“We will send a message to Harim,” she said evenly. “We will inform him that we have investigated the situation.”
She picked up a quill.
Parsons paused briefly, then nodded to herself.
“I will send one of my mages,” she continued calmly. “Someone Harim trusts.”
She lifted her gaze from the parchment.
“He will explain that there is no reason for panic,” she said matter-of-factly. “That the situation is under control, and that Stonegard is taking action.”
The quill touched the parchment lightly.
Hander drew a satisfied breath.
“This meeting is over,” he said in a firm voice. “You will take care of your people. We will handle the rest. Get yourselves back in order. In Stonegard, you are all welcome guests. But I warn you — I do not want to hear talk spreading through the castle about shadows and dark knights. Do we understand each other?”
For a moment, silence filled the hall.
“Do we understand each other?” Hander repeated, his gaze fixed firmly on the trio.
James stepped forward. “Yes,” he answered calmly. “We respect your conditions.”
He briefly glanced back at Aaliyah and Rowena. Neither of them spoke.
Aaliyah’s lips were pressed into a thin line, anger still burning in her eyes, but she nodded. Rowena stood quietly, pale but focused. DaVinci inclined his head almost imperceptibly — a gesture of agreement, not submission.
Hander nodded, satisfied.
“DaVinci, once your company has rested,” he said calmly, without command in his voice, “you should return to Castleton.”
DaVinci bowed formally and turned toward the door. The trio followed him. The heavy doors of the hall slowly closed behind them, muffling the voices inside.
The corridor was cool and silent.
“This is madness,” Aaliyah burst out. Her voice trembled with anger. “They don’t see it. They don’t want to see it!”
She turned to James.
“Zarveth is not just some mage with cheap tricks! We all know he—”
“Aaliyah.” James’s voice was quiet but firm.
“No!” she snapped, pulling away. “Because of their pride, many people could die.”
DaVinci stood aside, arms crossed over his chest. “You won’t convince them,” he said calmly. “Not today.”
Rowena remained silent. Fear lingered in her eyes.
DaVinci slowly removed his glove and rubbed his forehead. For a moment he closed his eyes, as if trying to gather his thoughts.
When he opened them again, his gaze was calm — but harder than before.
“Now it’s up to you,” he said quietly.
Aaliyah looked up at him immediately. “What do you mean?”
“Hander will have me watched,” he replied bluntly. “And once my company has rested, they will escort me out of the castle. Politely. With a smile.”
James nodded. “So no freedom of movement.”
“Exactly,” DaVinci answered. “That is why I cannot help you openly.”
He fell silent for a moment, then continued more firmly.
“Before I return to Castleton and begin preparing for the confrontation with Zarveth, I will first head back to the Sanctuary Oasis,” he said. “I will warn Harim. In person. Not through polished messages and comforting words.”
Rowena lifted her head. “And after that?”
“After that, I will also stop to see a friend at the Tower of Pure Light,” he replied. “He will listen.”
Aaliyah drew a breath, a flicker of hope appearing in her eyes. “So we’re not alone.”
“No,” DaVinci shook his head. “But do not count on Hander, Parsons, or Stonegard Castle to help you willingly. We must be prepared to fight even without them.”
Silence settled in the corridor.
“You stay here for now,” he said quietly.
Aaliyah grew alert.
He paused for a moment, then added more seriously:
“Zarveth is wounded,” he said calmly. “And even if he is not human, he still needs time to recover.”
His gaze moved from one to the next.
“Rest. Regain your strength,” he continued. “Because after that… time will no longer give us the chance to rest.”
Aaliyah nodded. “And then?”
“Then you begin to search,” he replied. “Find out everything you can — about the orb… and how to use it against the shadows.”
Silence lingered for a moment.
Footsteps echoed down the corridor before anyone could speak again.
They were calm. Measured. Unhurried.
James instinctively turned his head.
From around the corner appeared a servant in a dark castle livery. He bowed slightly, hands clasped before him, gaze respectfully lowered.
“Commander DaVinci,” he addressed him quietly. “Commander Hander has sent me to show you to your rooms.”
DaVinci nodded, as if he had expected it.
The servant then looked at the trio. “Please follow me.”
He turned and continued down the corridor. Their footsteps echoed again through the stone halls, softened by the thick castle walls.
They passed heavy doors, narrow staircases, and tall windows through which afternoon light streamed inside. Sunbeams broke against the stone edges and painted bright streaks along the walls, dust drifting within them.
The servant led them through the corridors until he stopped before massive doors. He turned toward them and inclined his head slightly.
“Before we proceed further,” he said calmly, “I have been instructed to deliver Commander Hander’s orders.”
DaVinci grew attentive.
“First, you will be examined and treated in the infirmary,” the servant continued matter-of-factly. “After that, you will proceed to the dining hall. Only then will you be allowed to retire to your rooms.”
DaVinci nodded, as if he had expected it.
“That is acceptable,” he said. “If I have been assigned the same room as last time, I can find it myself. I know the way.”
The servant bowed again. “Yes, sir. It is the same room.”
DaVinci nodded. “Good.”
He briefly turned to the trio. “We will meet again later,” he added quietly.
Then he took his leave with a simple nod, turned, and disappeared down one of the side corridors without needing further escort.
The servant resumed his role.
“Please,” he said to the trio, “come with me.”
He led them to the infirmary.
The air there was filled with the scent of herbs, alcohol, and resin. The healers were calm and focused, their touches quick and experienced. They examined and treated their wounds — nothing escaped their notice.
Once it was finished, the servant led them onward without unnecessary words.
The dining hall was bright, flooded with afternoon light. Warm food was already waiting on the tables. Simple, hearty fare — bread, meat, soup, water, and wine.
No one disturbed them.
Only after they had finished eating did the servant finally lead them to their rooms.
“You will be accommodated here,” he announced calmly. “If you require anything — clean clothes, food, drink — the servants are at your disposal.”
He bowed briefly. “You need only ask.”
Then he left, leaving them alone.
Behind the closed doors, silence settled.
Afternoon light streamed through the tall windows, casting warm bands across the floor.
The rooms were spacious and airy, furnished with comfortable, solid wooden furniture. Beautiful handwoven carpets spread beneath their feet, muffling every step. The walls were decorated with rich tapestries — scenes of battles, ancient castles, and the symbols of Stonegard, their colors still vivid despite their age.
Along one wall stood a large stone fireplace, now cold but ready to blaze again at any moment and drive away the chill of the castle walls. Vases filled with fresh flowers stood on tables and windowsills, their gentle fragrance softening the stern stone atmosphere.
Each room also had its own private bath — stone-built, spacious, with heated water and clean linen towels prepared for use.
Another quiet luxury that ordinary people rarely experienced. It was clear at first glance that these were not quarters for servants.
They were intended for more distinguished guests.
Such comfort was reserved only for those whom the castle wished not merely to host…but to impress.
