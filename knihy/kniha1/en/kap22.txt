Chapter XXII. - Rest before the shadow
The journey continued at a steady pace.
Since the clash with Zarveth, the landscape seemed to have taken a breath. For a brief moment, the group was able to feel a sense of calm. The sun no longer carried the cruelty of noon, and even the wind — though still bringing dust — had lost its restless tone.
DaVinci, the seasoned knight and commander of the expedition, led them forward in silence. His gaze constantly returned to the horizon, his body holding the calm of a warrior always prepared for the worst. Behind him rode the other knights, divided into smaller groups.
In the middle rode Aaliyah, Rowena, and James on borrowed horses.
Aaliyah sat upright, but her face betrayed exhaustion. The circles beneath her eyes had darkened, her gaze blurred, her lips pressed tight in an effort not to drift into sleep. Her magic had drained her completely, and though she spoke nothing of it, every step of the horse felt heavier than the last.
Beside her, Rowena swayed in the saddle.
Her head drooped, and several times she leaned so far that she had to be caught — once by James, then again by Aaliyah.
James kept close to them, silent, his body ready to act.
Whether against an unseen threat — or to catch one of the women again before she fell.
The knights maintained their pace. Conversation among them had faded. The silence was broken only by the thunder of hooves and the whisper of wind over the dunes. Meanwhile, the sun crossed its zenith, and the sky took on the copper hue of approaching evening.
Up ahead, DaVinci slowed.
He turned in the saddle, his gaze settling on the trio. He saw Rowena lean again, and James had to catch her for the third time within a single hour.
“Stop!” he called firmly. “We make camp here. We go no farther.”
One of the knights rode closer.
“Sir, if we want to reach Stonegard before nightfall, we should continue,” he said quietly. “Every hour works against us.”
DaVinci held his gaze.
“And every additional mile could cost them their lives,” he replied calmly, nodding toward the trio. “If they are to tell us what is happening, they must survive the journey.”
The knight pressed his lips together and nodded.
DaVinci scanned his men and pointed to two of them.
“You two. Ride ahead immediately. Warn the commander of the knights and the High Mage.”
Both knights straightened.
“Yes, sir.”
Without another word, they turned their horses and rode forward. Their silhouettes quickly shrank until they disappeared into the shimmering air above the sand.
James helped the women dismount.
Aaliyah leaned on her staff and closed her eyes for a moment.
The knights began setting up canvas shelters, building a fire, securing the horses. They worked quietly and efficiently.
DaVinci walked among them with calm steps, every gesture deliberate, every order clear. When he reached the small group, his gaze rested briefly on the women.
He saw their exhaustion and asked nothing.
He did not need to.
He read it in their faces, in every movement.
“Rest. We ride no farther today,” he said simply, then turned to his men. “Assign the watches. Silence does not mean safety. No one sleeps with both eyes closed.”
“I’ll take the first watch,” James said.
DaVinci turned toward him. “No. Rest.”
James shook his head. “I’ll be calmer if I stand guard. When I sleep, I cannot protect.”
The knight studied him in silence for a moment, then nodded. “As you wish. You can take the second watch.”

After sunset, Aaliyah sat by the fire with her staff resting across her lap. The flames cast shadows into her tired eyes, yet the hard glint of determination still remained within them.
Rowena carefully rubbed healing ointment onto her burned palms, while the orb near her glowed faintly once again.
The camp sank into silence.
The stars above shone sharply, almost cold.
The black sky wrapped around the group like a velvet veil, and the desert around them breathed with a chilling stillness. The crackling of the fire, the whisper of boots on gravel, and the clink of armor were the only sounds that remained.
When the first watch began, James rose and took his place at the edge of the camp. His silhouette blurred into the darkness. He stood motionless, eyes fixed on the distance.
Soon DaVinci joined him, without a word — as was his habit. For a while, they simply stood and watched the horizon.
The silence between them was not empty. It was the silence of men who had seen too much to speak lightly.
“Do you think he’ll return?” DaVinci asked quietly, without turning his head.
James remained silent for a moment, then answered softly: “Zarveth? He will return. But not now. Only when he is stronger… and when we are more tired.”
He tightened his grip on the hilts of his daggers.
From the desert, silence drifted once more — but it was not peace.
It was the silence before the storm.
