Chapter XXXII – The Game

Brienne quickly became an object of sympathy within the castle — and in some cases, even admiration. She was quiet, calm, polite. Grateful.

Too grateful.

William noticed her on the very first day. But he did not speak to her. He only watched.

On the first day, she sat alone in the library, pretending to read a volume about medicinal herbs. She turned the pages slowly, carefully, yet never took any notes. Any other student from the Tower would have been writing nonstop by now.

On the second day, she visited the infirmary. She helped. She cared for the patients. And yet — even after hours of work, her hands never trembled with fatigue. Her eyes observed more than the patient’s condition. They were alert. Scanning. Assessing.

On the third day, she seemed restless.

And that was when William decided it was time to discover what lay behind that charming mask.

Toward evening, he slipped away with a bottle of wine and two glasses. He knocked on her door as if it were nothing more than a casual gesture of gallantry.

“Who is it?” came a cautious voice.

“William. They told me you were here. And I thought… perhaps you might like some wine. For a quieter evening.”

A moment of silence.

Then came the soft click of the lock, and the door opened slightly.

Brienne stood in the doorway, wrapped in a simple cloak. She still appeared fragile.

But in her eyes was something different from uncertainty.

Calm.

Calculation.

“That is… kind,” she said, stepping aside to let him enter.

He walked in and poured wine for them both. They sat down — he in an armchair, she on the sofa.

“People say you survived something terrible,” he began quietly.
“And yet you seem… strong. Perhaps stronger than anyone would expect.”

“When you lose everything, you have no other choice,” she replied calmly.
“You either grow stronger… or you break.”

“You did not break.”

“Not yet.”

William narrowed his eyes. He drank, but did not abandon his vigilance.

He was aware that in this room, there were not two strangers.

There were two players.

Exactly what he had expected.

“I am curious how you escaped what killed the others,” he said quietly.

“Are you interested in survival… or in trust?” she asked gently.

He smiled.
“Perhaps both.”

Brienne remained silent for a moment. Then she began to tell her story — the same one as before, but this time with different emphasis. More feelings, fewer facts.

As if she were testing what would pass.

William listened, asked small questions, and gathered evidence between the lines. But he did not accuse her. He was too experienced for that.

Too hungry for power to risk unnecessary conflict.

When he left, he set his glass on the table, bowed with a faint smile, and departed without another word.

But that night, he returned.

Quietly.

Unannounced.

And in the room, he found Brienne standing by the window, her shadow stretched unnaturally far. The air in the chamber smelled of ash and something sharply metallic.

She said nothing, but she knew he was there.

She turned slowly, her eyes darkening.

“You knew,” she whispered.

“I suspected,” he corrected quietly.

And then it began — just a brief clash.

Brienne raised her hand, and shadows burst from her fingers with a venomous shimmer. William dodged, reacting quickly — but not before one of the tendrils slashed across his shoulder. The fabric tore.

And beneath it — on his skin — a black mark appeared.

An eye without a lens.
Surrounded by runes.
Darkness pulsing within it.

Brienne froze.

“Zarveth told me we had an ally here…” she breathed.
“But he never said who it was.”

The shadows in the room withdrew. Brienne looked away, and the tension dissolved into silence.

“So we are on the same side,” she said — half a question, half a realization.

“Apparently so,” William replied.

Then he smiled — not kindly, but victoriously.

“And now we finally know who we have the honor of dealing with.”
