Chapter XXXV – The Weight of Morning Silence

Thomas returned to his chamber slowly. Not because he was tired — but because every step seemed to force him to think about the same thing over and over again.

The dead boy.

The body lying in the cold underground. The hands too small. The legs too thin. The face too young. He was not a knight. Not a mage. Not even a healer. He was only a kitchen helper — a boy who should still have been alive, who should have been hauling sacks of flour that morning, complaining about waking early and sneaking pieces of leftover bread.

Instead, he lay dead.

Thomas clenched his jaw so tightly that it began to ache.

He knew killing. He had seen men fall in battle, heard the cries of the wounded and the dying. But this was not war. There was no enemy here with a sword in hand. Killing someone in battle was cruel, but understandable. War had rules, even if they were dirty ones.

But this?

This was corruption.
Impurity.

Someone had chosen a young, defenseless life. Destroyed it in the silence of the underground, beyond the eyes of witnesses, beyond honor, beyond any meaning. And the thought of it made Thomas’s stomach turn.

Disgust grew inside him — heavy and bitter.

How could someone enter Stonegard and commit such a thing? How could they pass through the castle walls, among guards and servants — and still remain unseen?

The knowledge that it had happened beneath their roof was almost unbearable.

As if someone had desecrated the very oath he had sworn.

He stopped by a narrow window and pressed his palm against the cold stone. The courtyard below seemed calm — too calm. Knights and squires rotated through their posts, servants woke to another day, the castle carried on as if nothing had happened.

And that was the worst part.

Because he knew something had happened.

Thomas closed his eyes.

For a moment, he imagined it had been someone else. Someone he knew. Someone he cared about. He pushed the thought away immediately.

A knight of Stonegard was meant to protect.
To stand between danger and the innocent.

And yet it had happened right beneath their feet.

Failure.

The word surfaced in his mind, even though he did not want to admit it.

He began walking again. His footsteps sounded muted, almost guilty. When he reached the door of his chamber, he opened it and stepped inside.

The room was quiet. Peaceful.

He removed his sword and set it against the wall, but the sound felt too loud. Too final.

He sat on the edge of the bed and stared into nothingness.

A young life. Needlessly destroyed. Without explanation. Without justice.

This was not just murder.

It was an insult to order.
A message carved into the stone of their castle.

And he refused to accept that it would go unanswered.

His fingers curled into a fist on their own.

Whoever it was — a dark mage, a fanatic, or something worse — had stepped into territory that was meant to be protected. Had killed the weak. Had hidden in the shadows.

A coward.

Thomas rose from the bed. The movement was slow, but determined.

He would not let this pass.

It was a matter of honor.

He stepped toward his sword, took it into his hand again, and for a moment let his palm rest on the hilt.

“I will find you,” he murmured, barely audible in the silence of the room.

It was a promise.
