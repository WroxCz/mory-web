Chapter IX. - Days from the ashes
Searching for the Key — James’s Perspective
A small boy, barely ten years old, pressed himself against the cold stone wall in one of the narrow back alleys of the city of Herby. Rain ran into his hair and soaked through the coat that hung loosely on his thin frame, dirty and far too large for him.
The city was loud even in darkness. The shouts of street vendors mixed with the clatter of horse hooves and the occasional cry of someone who had lost money, food — or simply their illusions.
James held a piece of soggy bread in his hands, something he had found behind one of the market stalls. He sat down on the ground in the shadows so no one would see him and quickly devoured his first meal in two days.
The cold stung his ears, but he tried not to make a sound. He knew that in Herby, weakness was something the city swallowed without mercy.
He watched the passersby carefully, noticing every movement, every glance. He learned quickly — who was friendly, who was dangerous, who might offer a starving child a scrap of food.
Even then, vigilance and the will to survive had already begun to grow inside him — qualities that would one day lead him down a path he could never have imagined.
Suddenly, he heard a rustle beside him.
James froze.
A few steps away crouched a boy about two years younger, black-haired, half-hidden behind discarded crates. His dark eyes were filled with hunger and fear.
Without a word, James extended his hand and offered him half of his bread.
The boy hesitated — then nodded and sat beside him in the shadows.
That day, they became “brothers.”
James watched over him, taught him how to survive — where to hide, how to move quickly, where to find food. The younger boy was slower, clumsier, easily distracted, yet James never grew angry with him.
One evening, after rain had left the streets slick and empty, they spotted a drunken man lying on the ground in a deserted alley.
The younger boy stopped for a moment, his eyes lighting up.
He saw opportunity.
He crept toward the man, intending to take the purse from his pocket — something they could trade for real food.
“Wait,” James whispered urgently, his voice low but firm. “It’s not safe. Stay behind me. I’ll go first.”
The younger boy frowned, but the determination in his eyes did not fade. He crouched beside the drunk and quickly reached into his coat.
As he tried to run back to James, proudly showing his prize, he tripped and fell.
The sound echoed through the quiet alley.
The man stirred.
His eyes snapped open, filled with fury, and his hand shot out, grabbing the boy.
The child struggled helplessly, but the drunken man was far stronger.
James froze — and in that moment, he knew he had to act.
There was no time to hesitate.
“No!” he shouted, throwing himself at the man.
The drunk released the younger boy and turned on James instead. The blows came hard and fast, pain exploding through his body, but James did not retreat.
In his eyes burned not only the instinct to protect — but defiance.
And that defiance caught the man’s attention.
He laughed, seized James by the throat, and lifted him into the air. James kicked wildly, even as pain spread through his body.
In the end, the man made a decision.
He grabbed both boys and dragged them into a shabby tavern where the air reeked of dampness, sweat, and cheap alcohol.
Still clutching them, he pushed his way to a table where a hooded man sat in the shadows.
His face was almost completely hidden — only his eyes glinted faintly in the dim light of a lamp.
The drunk leaned against the table and nodded toward the boys.
“I heard,” he slurred, his voice thick with alcohol, “that you’re looking for a pair of boys.”
The hooded man slowly raised his gaze and gave a brief nod.
The drunk leaned closer, grinning.
“I’ve got two boys for sale. Young, but capable. Feed them a bit, and you can do whatever you want with them.”
The hooded man lifted his head slightly.
“How much do you want for them?” he asked calmly.
The drunk thought for a moment, then smirked.
“Five gold coins.”
The hooded man slowly shook his head.
“They’re not worth that,” he said coldly, his voice emotionless. “I offer eight copper coins. Five for the older one, three for the younger.”
The drunk immediately grew angry.
“What? The older one fought me in the street!”
The hooded man merely smiled faintly, watching both boys without moving.
After a moment, he tossed eight copper coins onto the table.
“Take the money — or keep them,” he said calmly, in a voice that carried no mercy.
“Fine. The brats are yours now.”
The drunk snatched the coins and staggered toward the bar to buy another bottle, never looking back.
James and the younger boy stood helplessly — but James memorized everything.
Every blow.
Every look.
Every sound.
The hooded man leaned closer, his eyes still hidden beneath the shadow of his hood.
“Listen carefully,” he began, his voice deep and uncompromising. “The world you are entering is not like the streets you’ve wandered until now. Here, only those who learn to fight survive. Those who are not afraid of pain. Those who know their worth.”
James felt his heart racing, but the man continued in the same cold tone.
“Do not expect mercy. Do not expect anyone to lend you a helping hand. You will be trained. You will be tested. The strong will survive — and the weak will die.”
The boys stood silently at the table, tense and frightened. The younger boy clung to James, his fear trembling through his body.
The hooded man gave a brief, cold smile.
“But do not worry. If you endure and learn what is required, you will become something worthy of attention. Just remember: survival is a game where pain is the rule, and weakness is a luxury you cannot afford.”
Then he fell silent, nodded once, and stepped back into the shadows, his presence still hanging heavily over them.
James knew their lives had changed forever.
And that the road ahead would be harsh, merciless, and filled with darkness.

The hooded man rose, took hold of both boys, and led them through the streets of Herby, which seemed even colder and more hostile in the night.
James held the younger boy’s hand tightly. He felt the trembling in his fingers and knew he had to be strong now — not only for himself, but for him.
The journey was long and dark.
Narrow alleys gave way to abandoned courtyards and crumbling gates until they finally reached a tall stone gate with heavy iron bars.
The man led them through the main gate out of the city, along rocky paths between trees and cliffs, toward a distant estate.
When they arrived, they were not taken through the main entrance, but through a small back door hidden among the trees.
They did not go up into the mansion.
They went down.
Down a narrow stone staircase beneath the estate.
There, a mage and his assistant were already waiting.
The mage was short, round, and balding. His expression was friendly, but his eyes were sharp and cold — the eyes of a serpent that knew no mercy.
His assistant, quiet and watchful, studied the boys sternly through round spectacles.
“Welcome,” the mage said cheerfully, though his voice carried an unspoken threat.
“I hope you’re prepared.”
He stepped closer and began examining them — as if they were not people, but objects whose value and potential had to be carefully assessed.
His hands tested the firmness of their muscles, the flexibility of their joints, the tension in their bodies.
“Hm… they’re thin,” he muttered, running his fingers along the older boy’s arm.
“They don’t look like they’ll survive what awaits them. We may have to buy new ones if this fails.”
The hooded man stood nearby, silent and motionless, watching every movement of the mage.
“The older one,” he said at last calmly, “fought a drunk in the street. We’ll see what he can endure.”
The mage narrowed his eyes, slowly scanning James, then the younger boy.
His expression remained cold, analytical, devoid of compassion.
“Very well,” he murmured quietly. “We shall see.”

That very night, they were taken into a laboratory beneath the estate — a cold chamber filled with metal tables, cabinets of strange instruments, and the dim greenish glow of lamps.
The walls reeked of chemicals and dampness.
The first months of experiments were brutal.
Each day began before sunrise and ended long after sunset.
The mage tested them in many ways — sometimes physical, sometimes psychological. Every failed movement was recorded on parchment.
Dark rituals were performed on them and the other children they met there — every single day.
Before and after each ritual, the boys were forced to drink potions. Some clouded their minds. Others burned through their veins. Others made their hearts race so fast they could hear their own pulse pounding in their heads.
James was often bound in cold iron shackles, yet he barely felt the chill.
The only thing he felt was endless pain.
The younger boy suffered the same trials — but his body was smaller, weaker, more fragile.
So he felt everything more intensely.
As the days passed, the boys grew weaker, their bodies covered in bruises and scars.
Then came the day when the mage’s rituals took an even darker turn.
The boys watched as more and more children failed the tests.
Small bodies collapsed to the floor, their faces frozen in the agony they had felt before death.
Strange changes appeared on their skin.
Claws replaced fingernails.
Their flesh hardened like bark.
It was as if the ritual had tried to transform them — and failed.
James learned to focus on one thing.
Breath.
When the others panicked, he repeated the same rhythm to himself:
Inhale… exhale… inhale.
The beat of his heart became his refuge.
As long as he breathed, he lived.
And as long as he lived, he still had a chance.
“You see,” the mage murmured one day, watching the younger boy collapse beneath the weight of the trial, “weakness is not forgiven here.”
In his eyes burned the cold curiosity of a scientist observing an insect trapped in a jar.

Months passed.
James felt the younger boy changing.
Childlike helplessness faded. In his eyes remained only stubborn endurance. Pain had become his teacher, and fear a tool for survival.
The tests ended — at least the worst of them — but peace never came. Only change.
Instead of shackles, they were given weapons.
The daggers were dull, old, their edges chipped so badly they tore more than they cut. Every strike hurt their hands, every fall left a new scar. From a distance, the mage observed their training, recording everything on parchment — who attacked without hesitation, who defended, who hesitated.
“A hired killer is not a knight,” the masked man would say while the boys gasped for breath.
“Honor means nothing to him. He does not seek glory or recognition. He cares only about the result — a dead target and his own survival.”
James soon learned to recognize the rhythm of combat the same way he had once learned the rhythm of his breathing.
He watched.
He studied.
He noticed the movement of feet, the angle of strikes, the weakness in an opponent’s stance.
At first, the older boys beat him easily. But in time, he became the one others avoided.
He was not the biggest.
Not the strongest.
But he was fast.
And above all — calm.
The younger boy no longer trembled when he held a dagger. On the contrary, his fingers gripped it with certainty, as if it were part of his own hand. In his eyes remained only raw, untamed fury that flared whenever he smelled blood.
He was James’s complete opposite.
Where James chose patience, he attacked. Where James observed, he acted.
There was something wild inside him — something that refused to submit to order.
While James learned to kill out of necessity, the boy was already beginning to find a twisted satisfaction in it.
And the hooded man saw it.
Instead of suppressing it, he smiled.
Because that was exactly what he wanted them to become:
Two daggers.

The training was merciless.
At night, they were awakened by commands to kill shadows — illusions the mage conjured: people, animals, demons. Every movement had to be lethal. Every strike precise.
Those who hesitated were whipped.
Those who resisted were denied food.
Those who defied orders… disappeared.
No one asked where.
After some time, they became figures in black cloaks and black masks, moving silently like specters.
The masked man taught them that killing was not enough. They had to remain unseen, leave no trace, wait for the perfect moment.
“Killing,” he would say as he walked past them with his hands clasped behind his back,
“is not an art. The art is making sure no one ever knows it happened.”
And in the silent underground chambers, where the cries of dying children had once echoed, there was now only the sound of weapons striking wood and the muted breathing of those who had learned to survive.
James became one of the best.
Not because he wanted to.
But because he no longer knew how to be anything else.

Years passed.
The boys became young men.
They moved with a grace even seasoned swordsmen would envy — silent, precise, cold.
One day, the hooded man summoned them.
He stood beside a stone table in a room so quiet that the dripping of water from the ceiling could be heard. On the stone lay four black daggers.
Their blades gleamed as if they absorbed light rather than reflected it.
James and his brother stood opposite him.
They did not ask questions.
Both knew the time had come.
The hooded man slowly ran his fingers along one of the blades, as if stroking it.
“You have trained for a long time,” he said in a muffled voice, distorted by his mask.
“You have learned to survive. Now we will see whether you can do more than simply live.”
He extended his hand.
To each of them, he gave two daggers.
The cold metal felt heavier than expected.
“Tonight, you will kill for the first time. Not for me,” he continued, “but for yourselves. For the past that created you.”
James did not move.
He only tightened his grip on the handle.
His brother, however, smirked — that familiar predatory smile curling at the corner of his lips.
“Who?” he asked.
The hooded man paused for a moment.
“The one who sold you,” he answered simply.
The air grew heavy.
Even without words, they understood that this night they would not kill because of an order.
They would kill because of what had turned them into monsters.

The night they set out was cold.
Rain hammered against the roofs. The streets were empty.
The target lived in a small village beyond the city walls, in a decaying shack surrounded by the stench of alcohol.
He was a broken man.
Once brutal — now only a shadow of himself.
The brother sneered when he saw him through the cracked window.
“So this is what we survived for?” he whispered. “Pathetic.”
James did not answer.
His hand did not move.
He only watched as his brother crouched low and slipped silently inside.
A footstep.
A muffled cough.
Then — silence.
James entered a moment later.
Inside, the air smelled of blood and liquor.
The man lay on the floor. The brother stood above him, raising his dagger for another strike.
There was more than hatred in his eyes.
There was fury that had slept for years and now awakened — raw, uncontrollable, burning. It mixed with contempt and deep disgust.
His handsome face twisted, teeth clenched, breath ragged.
Each inhale was a cry of suppressed rage.
And James…stood still. Watching.
The hatred in his brother’s eyes reflected in his own — not with the same intensity, but like a shadow.
Cold. Quiet. Without passion.
“Enough,” James said softly.
The brother turned toward him. “Enough? I don’t know that word.”
The dagger struck again.
And again.
And again.
From the man who had once sold them, nothing remained but torn flesh and broken bones.
There was no battle.
No redemption.
Only violence returning to its source.
James watched. He did not try to stop him.
He only stood there, breathing — inhale, exhale — telling himself that this moment might be a beginning, not an end.
Outside, the rain continued to fall.
And when they stepped back into the darkness, their masks glistened with water and blood.
The brother, still breathing heavily, turned toward James.
James simply nodded.
His gaze beneath the mask was cold, unwelcoming — as if everything that had just happened were merely another step on the road, not the destination.

They returned to the Crimson estate through the back entrance — old, dust-covered doors hidden at the far end of the garden.
Rain drummed softly against the roofs, against leaves and brick, while their footsteps sounded almost silent, like shadows returning home.
In the underground chambers, a familiar room awaited them.
The door creaked as they entered.
The masked man sat at the table.
His mask hid his expression, but his presence was just as piercing as on the day he first saw them.
“Mission completed,” James said quietly, firmly.
His brother stood beside him, breathing fast, his eyes still burning with the remnants of rage.
The masked man leaned forward slightly, his hands resting on the table, watching them in silence.
“Good,” he said at last, calm but authoritative. “This was only the beginning. Your second task awaits.”
The brother straightened, his hand instinctively dropping to his dagger.
“Second task?” he asked, his voice rough, edged with tension.
Their eyes met.
In the silence that followed, both knew the night was not over.
The masked man tilted his head.
“Yes. The first task was only a test. This is the real mission.”
James nodded.
His expression remained calm, emotionless.
But the brother tightened his grip on the dagger, ready to face whatever came.
“Everything you have done so far was only preparation,” the masked man continued. “Now you must show more. This is… a test of character.”
Silence filled the underground chamber.
The air felt heavy — like the moment before a storm.
Both brothers understood.
There would be no return to a normal life.
The real game was just beginning.
The brother snorted softly, almost mocking. “We’re ready… though I hope this time we won’t have to drown in the blood of strangers.”
The masked man laughed quietly.
The sound was deep, cold, echoing against the stone walls.
“No,” he said at last. “This time… it won’t be foreign blood.”

The masked man straightened and nodded. “Follow me.”
They descended a narrow stone staircase deeper into the underground.
The steps ended, opening into a long, damp corridor. Its walls were dark, covered in dust and moss. Smaller side passages branched from it — narrow and twisting, leading nowhere… or perhaps everywhere.
The masked man moved with certainty.
He knew every turn, every corner, as if the underground itself belonged to him.
The brothers stayed close behind.
Their eyes drifted more often toward the shadows in the side corridors than toward his back.
Each passage felt like a trap.
Each corner — a threat waiting to strike.
After several minutes that seemed endless, they entered a larger chamber. From there, wide doors opened into a training arena. Torches illuminated the stone floor and wooden figures carved for practice. High above them stretched a balcony. On it stood a group of masked figures, watching silently.
Observing. Waiting.
The masked man stopped. His voice broke the silence. “This is your true test. You must face each other. The one who survives will become part of the Crimson Order. The one who falls… will disappear forever.”
The brothers froze. Their breathing quickened. Their eyes met.
In that look was shock, disbelief, disgust — and hidden terror.
“What… what do you mean?” the brother whispered hoarsely. “Us… against each other?”
The masked man nodded once. “Yes. Your skill. Your strength. Your resolve. Everything must be proven now. The one who survives will join us. The other… will not.”
James stood perfectly still. As if the words were merely another step on a road he had always known he would walk.
But the brother tightened his grip on the dagger. His heart pounded violently in his chest.
“That… that’s impossible,” he breathed. “We can’t… he’s my brother…”
“That is irrelevant,” the masked man said coldly. “When you step onto the arena, each of you stands alone. The rules are simple: survive, and you become one of us.”
On the balcony, the masked figures remained silent.
Their presence was a quiet witness to the merciless fate hanging over the brothers.
James slowly turned toward his younger brother. His face remained calm — unnaturally calm — as if every storm inside him had suddenly gone quiet.
In his eyes was something the brother did not expect: Resolve. Coldness. And quiet compassion.
“Kill me,” James said softly. “And live.”
The brother stared at him, stunned. “What?”
“Listen to me,” James continued, even calmer. “If you want to live — if you want a chance — you have to do it. This is the price.”
His voice was not pleading.
Not desperate.
It was final. As if he had calculated the sacrifice long ago.
The younger brother felt something twist inside his chest — anger toward the masked man, betrayal, and beneath it all, gratitude for everything James had done for him.
Above them, someone on the balcony laughed. Short. Cold.
Time shrank into a single moment.
The younger brother took a step forward.
Slowly. Hesitantly — as if every movement hurt.
He took a second step, then a third… and between them spread a silence in which only the dripping of water and the beating of two hearts could be heard.
James did not move.
He stood motionless, the daggers still at his belt, making no attempt to defend himself. His gaze was calm. Resigned.
Ready to die if it meant his brother would live.
The younger brother raised his dagger — his hand trembling.
He took another step… and then he ran.
The cry that tore from his lips was a mixture of despair and rage at the whole world. He ran straight toward James — but just before reaching him, in the final moment, he changed direction.
Instead of his brother, he lunged at the masked man.
The dagger flashed in the torchlight — and then struck.
Once. Twice. Again and again.
The masked man did not even have time to scream — stunned, unable to comprehend that the rules of his own game had just collapsed.
Blood splattered across the stone floor, droplets falling like echoes of a decision that could never be taken back.
The younger brother straightened, the dagger still in his hand, his breathing slowly calming.
Then came his laughter.
Deep. Raw. Filled with fury and release.
At first short and mocking, then growing louder, until the entire underground arena echoed with his defiance.
“You think I’ll play your game?” he shouted toward the masked figures on the balcony, his voice trembling with anger and irony. “You want me to kill my own brother just to survive? Go to hell! I’d rather die too!”
The masked figures froze.
Some tightened their grip on their weapons. Others simply stood there, stunned and shaken.
Silence fell over the hall — thick enough to cut.
James stood motionless, eyes wide with shock, breath held.
Then laughter came from the balcony.
Deep. Genuine. Pleased.
The man who had been directing the entire scene leaned forward over the railing and laughed loudly.
“I like you, boys,” he called out with satisfaction. “You’re like a pair of daggers. One is good — but two are better. You will both live.”
The air shifted.
The tension dissolved into a strange, heavy astonishment.
The younger brother took a deep breath. His laughter faded, and on his lips appeared a mixture of relief and pride.
The air in the underground trembled.
The masked man’s words still echoed through the chamber as both brothers stood silently, unable to fully comprehend what they had just heard.
“You may go,” the man added calmly — a tone that did not match the sight of his dead companion’s body. “To your quarters. Rest. Tomorrow begins a new day.”
The brothers bowed and walked down the corridor.
Their footsteps sounded hollow.
Water dripped from the ceiling in rhythm with their breathing.
No one spoke.
The silence between them was heavy — filled with unspoken fear, guilt, and an incomprehensible calm after the storm.

When they entered the barracks, the air felt heavier than anywhere else.
Other young men sat on their beds — exhausted, their eyes empty, all dressed in the same dark clothing.
When they saw the brothers, they lifted their heads.
And in that moment, a grave-like silence filled the room.
“How… is that possible?” one of them breathed. “We… we had to kill our companions.”
James looked at him quietly, without pride.
“The test isn’t about blood,” he said slowly. “It’s about choice. About whether you let yourself be broken — or whether you keep standing.”
The words fell among them like stones into water.
In the others’ eyes swirled confusion, anger, and guilt.
And then it happened — an explosion.
One of the young men, his hand trembling, his gaze wavering between despair and madness, shouted:
“It’s not fair!” He pulled out a dagger with a shaking hand and charged straight at James.
“You said it’s about choice? Then now I’ll make mine!” he screamed, his voice full of hysteria and pain.
James did not have time to move.
He only saw his younger brother leap in front of him.
A swift motion.
A flash of steel.
Then impact.
The dagger sank deep.
“No!” James shouted, catching him before he hit the ground.
Blood spread between his fingers — warm, too real.
“No, no, no, not this—”
The younger brother trembled.
His eyes lifted to James.
Everyone around them froze.
No one moved.
Time stopped.
“You mustn’t… blame yourself,” he whispered, his voice fading, but his gaze clear. “You were willing to die to protect me. Now I’ve just… returned the favor.”
James felt his heart pounding — but something shifted in his mind. The world around him began to dissolve. Torchlight fractured. Shadows blurred and lost their shape.
“What…?” he breathed.
But his brother only smiled.
He stood up — even though his body should not have been able to — and looked him straight in the eyes.
“You have to go, James,” he said quietly, pointing toward a door that appeared out of the darkness as if from nowhere.
“This… wasn’t real,” James whispered.
“It was,” his brother replied calmly. “They are your memories. But there is nothing to regret. Now go… and live.”
James looked at the door — and at his brother.
He smiled one last time.
Peaceful. Almost reconciled.
James took a step.
And walked through the door.

Searching for the key — from Aaliyah’s perspective
Aaliyah lay on silk bedding, its cool softness contrasting with the inner heat that still surrounded her.
Morning was dim. Light barely filtered through the tent walls. The air smelled of incense.
He was gone. As always.
William.
Beautiful and dangerous like lightning. His deep brown eyes were cold as ice. At night, they whispered her name softly and urgently, like a prayer no one else was allowed to hear.
In the morning, they looked at her like an empty vessel — something that existed only for him to pour his desire into and discard.
Memories of their first meeting struck her again.
It had been in Vienta, during the festival, when the streets pulsed with colors, the scent of spices, and the rhythm of drums. The city lived in joyful chaos. But she felt that her own life was about to turn upside down.
William had come under orders from his lord to visit the local fire mages. He needed their power and knowledge for a mission in the unforgiving desert.
When their eyes met for the first time, it felt like the collision of two blazing worlds. William’s firm charisma and cold seriousness contrasted with Aaliyah’s wild energy and the fire that burned around her like an untamed storm. Yet in that first moment, something deeper than cooperation was born.
“There is fire burning inside you,” he said in a low voice that allowed no resistance. “It’s a heat that could burn everything around you. That flame — I want to control it. Tame it. Bind it and use it as I wish. This is not just about power. It’s about power over you — over what burns within you. You are wild, untamed… and I am the one who will decide when and how that fire is unleashed.”
His words burned her more than any flame.
She blushed — but he only smiled.
Gently.
Dangerously.
Like a hunter who had just captured his prey.
Their nights were like a secret ritual — crushing her and keeping her alive at the same time.
William’s body was firm and muscular.
Every movement precise. Unyielding.
He touched her with irresistible certainty — one hand sliding along her neck, the other gripping her waist, while his lips caressed and his teeth bit, as if he wanted to burn his mark into her skin.
Aaliyah opened herself to him — her wildness and her fragility.
Their movements merged into a fierce, chaotic dance where every breath carried expectation and fear, every touch pulsed like a storm that could not be stopped.
His kisses turned pain into pleasure.
But when morning came, William was gone.
Or he was there — but only as a cold shadow.
Impenetrable. Distant.
Aaliyah wrote him letters. The answer was always silence. A silence that burned more than his words ever could.
He had never kissed her.
When she once dared to press her lips to his, he pulled away coldly and whispered: “I don’t like that.”
His words were like an icy blow that shattered every hope.
Yet deep inside, she kept believing that one day it would change.
Beside her on the pillow lay a note. Simple. Cold. Without emotion.
It’s over.
Words that sounded like a quiet sentence.
How could she love someone who discarded her like this?
Who never truly saw her?
Who took her — but never gave her anything beyond fleeting passion?
And still… she clung to a fragile hope that something might change.
That she might change him.
The feeling of abandonment tightened around her like an iron grip. She sat up, breathing heavily. But no tears came. Only heavy silence.
An emptiness that surrounded her like cold fog.
“I loved him,” she whispered into the void.
No one answered.
“I wanted him to see me as a person. Not as a toy he chooses when he’s tired of fighting.”
Then, in the corner of the tent, a figure appeared.
Dark — but not threatening.
It was James.
His voice was quiet, firm, filled with tenderness.
“Aaliyah… I love you. I expect nothing in return. I will never try to control your fire. I will protect you, even if you don’t want me to. Because true love means being there — even when we are unsure, doesn’t it?”
Aaliyah froze.
His words pierced deep inside her. Shattering the ice around her heart.
James leaned closer. His gaze was full of sincerity and warmth. Gently, he placed his hand on her cheek. And kissed her.
Softly.
Tenderly.
Yet with a passion that ignited her entire heart.
And just as quietly as he had come, the shadow of James slowly disappeared — fading into the morning light, leaving behind a sense of peace and new possibilities.
Aaliyah remained sitting on the bed. Her breathing slowly steadied. Her heart felt lighter.
She realized how many emotions she had wasted on William.
How much energy she had sacrificed to a love that crushed her.
And now?
Now it was time to leave the past behind. And find her true happiness.
Slowly, she stood, put on a simple dress, and stepped with determination out of the tent. Suddenly, she found herself once again in the old, ruined temple where time stood still.
James and Rowena were already waiting for her.
Their gazes were calm and steady — filled with quiet support and understanding.
Rowena stood with her hands clasped, her expression peaceful yet radiating strength and wisdom.
James stood beside her like a protective shadow, his eyes shining with unwavering devotion.
Aaliyah felt a new strength slowly rising within her — as if their presence wrapped around her like a warm cloak of hope.
For the first time in a long while…she was not alone.
