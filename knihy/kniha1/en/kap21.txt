Chapter XXI. - The crossing
The road ahead led across uneven terrain, where sand gradually mixed with sharp boulders and ancient rocks whose surfaces were covered in faded carvings. The sun climbed higher, and the sky took on an unhealthy pale shade.
Commander DaVinci, riding at the head of the expedition, raised his hand. The column halted. “There used to be a watchtower here. The ruins stand just beyond the gorge. We should be careful.”
James moved closer. “You expect an ambush?”
“No. But I can feel that something remains. Not from people.” His fingers tightened around the reins.
Rowena narrowed her eyes. She dismounted and pulled the orb from her satchel. Its glow spread across the stones like ripples on water. “There’s residual magic here,” she said calmly.
“It isn’t alive. But… it’s as if something powerful passed through and left traces behind. Invisible… almost.”
Aaliyah lifted her staff, its flames burning quietly. “We’re close to the epicenter. This place knows pain.”
The knights spread into a defensive formation. Their horses neighed uneasily, and one young rider had to grip his saddle when his mount suddenly stepped back in panic.
“We take a break,” Commander DaVinci decided. “But we stay in armor.”
The trio dismounted and walked slowly among the stones, cracked by heat and time.
Rowena placed her hand on a rock. Beneath her palm, it pulsed with a faint echo of ancient pain.
Her vision darkened for a moment — a flash.
A vision.
A dark knight — Zarveth — kneeling in the dust, whispering unknown words into the cracked earth.
And then a scream… not human.
“Did you see something?” Aaliyah asked, noticing how Rowena staggered.
“Yes… perhaps remnants of a ritual.”
At that moment, the wind rose. Not violently — but insistently.
It crept between the rocks, hissed through their cracks, and carried with it the stench of decay.
The horses reacted instantly. Several reared. Others began backing away, snorting in panic, as if trying to flee an unseen threat. One knight barely held onto the reins as his horse tried to bolt.
DaVinci sharpened his focus.
“Hold them!” he ordered.
The animals sensed something human eyes had not yet seen.
And then it happened.
Not far from them, among the boulders at the edge of the gorge, something moved.
Not quickly.
Slowly.
Heavily.
A figure stepped out from the shadow of the rocks, one step after another, as if something forced it forward long after it had lost the reason to walk.
An undead.
Fragments of armor and a torn red cloak hung from its shoulders. Its skin was shriveled and cracked, in places completely missing, exposing bone. Its head tilted unnaturally to one side, empty eye sockets staring through the world — not at them, but somewhere beyond.
It walked. Just walked.
The sand whispered beneath its feet, each step sounding like fingernails scraping against stone.
James instinctively reached for his daggers, but DaVinci signaled for him to wait.
“Not yet,” he whispered.
Rowena felt a chill run down her spine. The orb at her side remained dark, but the air around the undead carried the same corrupted echo she had sensed in her vision.
Aaliyah tightened her grip on her staff.
“He’s not alone,” she said quietly.
The undead slowly disappeared behind a rocky outcrop.
For a moment, silence fell.
Only the wind hissed again between the stones, and the horses stomped nervously.
DaVinci scanned the expedition, his expression hard and resolute. Then he dismounted, landing firmly on the ground.
“We split here,” he said quietly, yet loud enough for all to hear.
He pointed toward the horses. “You stay here. Keep the animals together and be ready to move. The rest of you — with me.”
Several knights nodded and took the reins, while others dismounted beside him.
DaVinci turned to the trio. “You come with us.”
James nodded silently and drew his daggers.
Aaliyah shifted her staff into her other hand.
Rowena pulled her cloak closer around her body, her eyes fixed on the place where the undead had vanished.
“We move quietly,” DaVinci continued. “I want to know where he’s going. He won’t be alone. He wears the cloak and armor of the knights of Stonegard. They never travel the desert in groups smaller than three.”
He gave a brief signal.
The group moved forward, their steps muffled by sand and shadow.
Behind them remained the horses and part of the knights, ready to intervene at any moment.
The gorge swallowed them, air grew colder and sounds of the desert faded. The walls rose above them like dark ramparts, and the sunlight narrowed into thin, cold beams.
Ahead, he appeared again.
The undead.
He walked slowly, with strange certainty, as if he knew the path better than the living.
Then suddenly, he disappeared behind a rocky ledge.
DaVinci raised his hand.
They stopped.
A dull scraping sound followed. Then another.
When they carefully rounded the rock, a small rocky hollow opened before them — a natural depression whose floor was scattered with bones and broken fragments of armor.
At its center stood a stone.
A tall, irregular monolith, completely smeared with dried blood that ran down its sides in dark, sticky streaks. The sand around it had hardened and blackened, as if blood had soaked into it again and again over time.
Around the stone stood undead knights.
They did not move forward.
They did not attack.
They simply stood in a circle.
Their armor was damaged, their cloaks torn, their heads bowed. Their bodies swayed slowly from side to side in a rhythm that could not be heard — but could be felt.
Like the heartbeat of something that should have long since stopped.
The air was heavy. Suffocating.
Thick with old blood and corrupted magic.
Rowena felt her stomach tighten. The echo of pain she had sensed in the rock was stronger here — concentrated.
“They’re bound to that stone,” she whispered. “It holds them here. Like witnesses… or sacrifices.”
DaVinci gripped the hilt of his sword and stepped forward.
Resolve flashed in his eyes.
“Destroy them,” he whispered.
But James grabbed his arm instantly. “No.”
The commander turned to him, surprised by the tone. “Why not?”
James scanned the surroundings cautiously. “We need to leave. If this is where it began… he may return here.”
DaVinci frowned. The lines on his forehead deepened.
“If we destroy them now,” James continued quietly, eyes still fixed on the circle of undead,
“Zarveth will realize we found this place. He’ll move on and start again — with other bodies. Other victims. And next time, he’ll erase the evidence.”
He paused.
“But if we leave, he’ll have no reason to change anything. He’ll come back here. And in the meantime, we gain time. We prepare. We plan a killing strike.”
For a moment, only the slow swaying of the bodies could be heard. DaVinci stood motionless. He stared at the monolith for a long time, as if searching it for an answer. “So we let him believe he’s safe.”
“Yes,” James replied. “When we strike, we destroy him.”
The commander clenched his jaw, but his voice remained calm. “Good.”
He gave a quiet signal to withdraw. “Remember this place. We will return.”
The group retreated along the same path they had entered, their steps slow and careful, without a single unnecessary sound.
No one looked back.
Yet all of them felt the gaze of the blood-stained monolith on their backs.
When they left the gorge, the air warmed again, and the light spread across the land.
The horses greeted them with restless stamping and soft whinnies, as if they too felt relief.
DaVinci mounted last. He cast one final glance toward the dark rocks. “One more day to Stonegard,” he said. “We ride faster.”
The expedition moved forward once more.
Beyond the gorge, the first stone outcroppings of the castle were already visible.
Its towers were low but strong. The fortress was not beautiful. But it was unbreakable.
Built not for comfort —but for survival.
And now, it was their last hope.
